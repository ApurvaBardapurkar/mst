02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Swiggy Co-op That Could Have Been a DAO

📖 Story

In 2023, the delivery partners of a popular food delivery app in India were unhappy. The platform took a 25% commission from restaurants, paid delivery riders a fixed wage with no say in policy, and could deactivate accounts overnight. The riders had no voice. A community organiser proposed a radical idea: what if the riders, restaurants, and customers collectively owned the platform? Instead of a CEO setting fees, they would vote on commission rates. Instead of a central team deciding who gets promoted, reputation tokens earned through reliable deliveries would grant voting power. The platform's code would enforce the rules - not a manager.


This is the vision of a Decentralized Autonomous Organization. While the Swiggy co-op hasn't been built yet, similar DAOs already govern billions of dollars. MakerDAO, a DAO of MKR token holders, controls the DAI stablecoin - deciding interest rates, collateral types, and risk parameters. Uniswap's DAO manages a multi-billion dollar treasury. ConstitutionDAO, a short-lived experiment, raised $47 million in a week to bid on a rare copy of the U.S. Constitution. DAOs are not theoretical; they are live, functioning, and growing.

03   Background & History

From "The DAO" to MakerDAO - The Rise of Code-Governed Organizations

The concept of a decentralized autonomous organization predates the term itself. In 2013, Daniel Larimer, creator of BitShares, coined the phrase "Decentralized Autonomous Company" to describe an entity where shareholders vote on decisions using tokens. But the world took notice in 2016 with the launch of "The DAO" on Ethereum - a venture capital fund where anyone could pitch a project and token holders would vote on investments. It raised $150 million in Ether, the largest crowdfunding in history at the time. Within weeks, a hacker exploited a reentrancy vulnerability and drained $60 million. The Ethereum community hard-forked to recover the funds, splitting the chain into Ethereum and Ethereum Classic. "The DAO" was a spectacular failure, but it proved one thing: code could govern money at scale. It also taught the world that code must be bulletproof.

From those ashes rose a more cautious, better-engineered generation. MakerDAO, launched in 2017, created a DAO to govern the DAI stablecoin. MKR token holders vote on risk parameters, stability fees, and new collateral types. MakerDAO has survived market crashes, regulatory scrutiny, and internal disputes - proving that a well-designed DAO can manage real financial risk.

The DeFi summer of 2020 supercharged DAO adoption. Compound, Uniswap, Aave, and dozens of other protocols distributed governance tokens to users, transforming them from passive consumers into active governors. Today, DAOs manage over $25 billion in treasuries, fund public goods (Gitcoin), buy rare collectibles (ConstitutionDAO), and even attempt to buy sports teams (Krause House). On MST, with its sub-paisa fees and hybrid architecture, a new generation of DAOs can emerge - ones where every small vote is recorded on-chain without economic friction.

Key insight: A DAO is not just a "company on the blockchain." It's a new organizational primitive - global, permissionless, transparent, and unstoppable by any single party. The code is the CEO.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Vending Machine Democracy - How a DAO Works in Plain English

🤖 The Analogy

Imagine a vending machine that the whole neighborhood owns. Every resident has a few tokens. Want to change the price of cola? Anyone can propose it. The proposal goes to a vote. If a majority of token holders vote yes, the vending machine's internal computer automatically adjusts the price - no manager needed. The machine's earnings are deposited into a shared bank account (the treasury), and token holders vote on what to do with the profits: reinvest in more snacks, donate to the local school, or distribute to token holders. The machine runs 24/7, transparently, and no single person can override the rules. That's a DAO - but instead of cola, it might be managing a $5 billion stablecoin.

Every DAO has five core components. Let's understand each in simple terms:

T
TOKEN
The Governance Token - Your Voice in the DAO
A governance token is a special ERC-20 token that represents voting power. Holding 1% of the token supply means you have 1% of the votes. Tokens can be earned by contributing to the DAO (working, providing liquidity) or purchased on the open market. Owning tokens aligns your interests with the DAO's success - if the DAO thrives, the token value increases. This is the "share" of the DAO world, but with governance rights explicitly programmed in.
V
VOTE
Voting Mechanism - How Decisions Are Made
DAOs use various voting systems: simple token voting (1 token = 1 vote), quadratic voting (the cost of additional votes increases quadratically, reducing whale influence), delegation (token holders assign their votes to trusted experts), and conviction voting (votes accumulate over time, favoring long-term commitment). The most common today is token-weighted voting with delegation - like a representative democracy where your representative's power depends on how many tokens are delegated to them.
P
PROPOSAL
Proposal System - How Ideas Become Actions
Anyone can submit a proposal to the DAO, though typically a minimum token holding or sponsorship is required to prevent spam. A proposal might be: "Invest 1,000 USDC from the treasury into Protocol X" or "Change the interest rate on DAI from 3% to 5%." The proposal includes a description, a voting period, and the executable code (the smart contract function call that will be executed if the proposal passes). Once submitted, the voting period begins.
E
EXECUTE
Execution - When Code Takes Action
If a proposal reaches quorum (minimum votes) and a majority votes in favor, it passes. But it doesn't execute immediately - a timelock (e.g., 48 hours) gives token holders time to exit if they disagree. After the timelock, anyone can call the execute() function, which runs the pre-approved code. This might transfer funds from the treasury, call another smart contract, or update a protocol parameter. The execution is atomic and unstoppable - the code runs exactly as specified.
💰
TREASURY
The Treasury - The DAO's Shared Bank Account
A DAO's treasury is a multisig wallet or a smart contract that holds the DAO's assets - tokens, stablecoins, NFTs. The treasury is controlled by the governance process: no single person can move funds; only a passed proposal can trigger a transfer. This prevents rug pulls by founders. The treasury is funded by protocol fees, token sales, or donations. Transparent on-chain treasuries allow anyone to audit the DAO's finances in real time.
05   Technical Deep Dive - A Minimal DAO Contract

Build a Simple DAO in 50 Lines of Solidity

Let's implement a bare-bones DAO to see how these components translate into code. This contract uses OpenZeppelin's Governor and TimelockController - the industry standard.

// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity ^0.8.20;

import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/Governor.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorCountingSimple.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorVotes.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorVotesQuorumFraction.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorTimelockControl.sol";

contract MyDAO is Governor, GovernorCountingSimple, GovernorVotes, GovernorVotesQuorumFraction, GovernorTimelockControl {
    constructor(IVotes _token, TimelockController _timelock)
        Governor("MyDAO")
        GovernorVotes(_token)
        GovernorVotesQuorumFraction(4) // 4% quorum
        GovernorTimelockControl(_timelock)
    {}

    function votingDelay() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 1 days; }
    function votingPeriod() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 3 days; }

    // The following functions are overrides required by Solidity.
    function proposalThreshold() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 10_000e18; } // 10,000 tokens to propose
    function quorum(uint256 blockNumber) public view override(Governor, GovernorVotesQuorumFraction) returns (uint256) {
        return super.quorum(blockNumber);
    }
}

This single contract includes: voting with a governance token, a 4% quorum, a 1-day voting delay, a 3-day voting period, and a timelock for execution. All of this is inherited from OpenZeppelin - battle-tested and audited. To deploy, you also need a governance token (an ERC-20 with ERC-20Votes extension) and a TimelockController.

05A   Voting Mechanisms Deep Dive

How DAOs Actually Decide - Token Voting, Quadratic Voting, and Beyond

The voting mechanism is the beating heart of any DAO. Choose the wrong one, and your DAO devolves into a plutocracy or gridlock. Choose the right one, and you unlock genuine collective intelligence. Let us explore the major voting mechanisms in depth, understand their trade-offs, and see which DAOs use each one.

1. Token-Weighted Voting (1 Token = 1 Vote)

This is the simplest and most widely used mechanism. Each governance token entitles the holder to one vote. If you hold 10,000 tokens out of 1,000,000 total, you control 1% of voting power. MakerDAO, Uniswap, and Compound all use this model. It is straightforward to implement using OpenZeppelin's GovernorCountingSimple extension.

The problem: Token-weighted voting concentrates power among large holders ("whales"). In practice, a handful of venture capital firms and early team members often hold 30-60% of a governance token's supply. They can pass any proposal they wish, effectively recreating the corporate board they sought to replace. During Uniswap's fee switch debate, a16z's voting power alone was larger than thousands of small holders combined.

2. Quadratic Voting (QV)

Quadratic voting addresses the whale problem mathematically. Instead of 1 token = 1 vote, the cost of each additional vote increases quadratically. Your first vote costs 1 token. Your second vote costs 4 tokens total (2 squared). Your third costs 9 tokens. Your tenth costs 100 tokens. This means a holder with 100 tokens gets 10 votes, while a holder with 10,000 tokens gets only 100 votes - not 100 times more, but only 10 times more. QV ensures that the breadth of support matters more than the depth of any single holder's wallet.

Gitcoin Grants uses a variant of quadratic voting called Quadratic Funding to allocate matching funds. Projects that receive small donations from many people get disproportionately more matching funds than projects funded by a few large donors. This has directed millions of dollars to open-source projects that would otherwise struggle to get funded.

Indian Context

Think of quadratic voting like India's Panchayati Raj system, where every village gets representation regardless of population size. A large city (whale) does not get to override the voices of many small villages (retail holders). The principle is the same: broad participation matters more than concentrated power. Similarly, Indian cooperative banks follow a "one member, one vote" rule under the Banking Regulation Act - no matter how much capital you deposit, your vote counts the same as every other member's. Quadratic voting is a mathematical middle ground between the cooperative model and pure token-weighted voting.

3. Conviction Voting

Conviction voting, pioneered by Commons Stack and used by 1Hive's Gardens, is a continuous voting mechanism where your vote accumulates "conviction" over time. Instead of a fixed voting period, you signal your preference and leave your tokens committed. The longer you keep your tokens staked on a proposal, the more conviction it builds. Once conviction crosses a threshold (which depends on the amount being requested from the treasury), the proposal passes automatically.

This mechanism favors long-term, committed community members over speculators who buy tokens just before a vote. It also eliminates the need for discrete voting periods - proposals can pass at any time as they accumulate enough community conviction. The trade-off is complexity: it is harder for newcomers to understand, and the mathematical threshold function needs careful calibration to avoid proposals passing too quickly or too slowly.

4. Rage Quit

The rage quit mechanism, introduced by Moloch DAO, is not a voting method per se but a critical protection for minority holders. If a proposal passes that you strongly disagree with, you can "rage quit" before it executes - burning your governance tokens and withdrawing your proportional share of the treasury. This gives every member an exit option and prevents the majority from expropriating minority assets.

Moloch DAO was specifically designed for grant-giving, where members pool funds and vote on grants. If a proposal passes to fund a project you consider wasteful, you rage quit and take your money back. This keeps the DAO honest - members will not propose reckless spending if they know others will simply leave and take their share of the treasury with them. The mechanism is named after the ancient deity associated with sacrifice, reflecting the idea that leaving the DAO is a sacrifice of community membership.

5. Snapshot (Off-Chain) Voting

Not all voting needs to happen on-chain. Snapshot is a popular off-chain voting tool where token holders sign messages (using their wallet) to cast votes without paying gas fees. The results are stored on IPFS, not on the blockchain. Snapshot votes are typically used for "temperature checks" or non-binding polls before a formal on-chain proposal is submitted. Over 20,000 DAOs use Snapshot, including Aave, ENS, and SushiSwap.

The limitation is that Snapshot votes are non-binding - the DAO's smart contract does not enforce the result automatically. A separate on-chain transaction is needed to execute the outcome. However, for DAOs on MST, on-chain voting is so inexpensive that Snapshot may become unnecessary - every vote can be directly on-chain, providing both the ease of Snapshot and the enforceability of on-chain governance.

07A   DAO Legal Structures

Giving a DAO a Legal Identity - From Wyoming to India

A DAO exists on the blockchain, but it must interact with the real world - signing leases, opening bank accounts, paying contributors, and dealing with tax authorities. Without a legal wrapper, a DAO is often treated as a general partnership, meaning every token holder could be personally liable for the DAO's debts and legal obligations. This section explores how DAOs acquire legal personhood across different jurisdictions and what options exist for Indian builders.

Wyoming DAO LLC (United States)

In 2021, Wyoming became the first U.S. state to recognize DAOs as a distinct type of Limited Liability Company. Under Wyoming's DAO LLC law (W.S. 17-31-101 through 17-31-116), a DAO can register as an LLC where the operating agreement is supplemented or even replaced by smart contracts. Members enjoy limited liability protection, meaning their personal assets are shielded from the DAO's liabilities. The DAO must designate a registered agent in Wyoming and can be either "member-managed" (all token holders vote) or "algorithmically managed" (smart contracts handle operations autonomously). Several well-known DAOs, including CityDAO, have registered under this framework.

Foundation Model (Cayman Islands, Switzerland)

Many large DeFi protocols use offshore foundations as their legal wrapper. The Cayman Islands Foundation Company is popular because it does not have shareholders - it has "supervisors" who oversee the foundation's directors. This structure aligns well with a DAO where no single party "owns" the entity. The foundation can hold intellectual property, enter contracts, and employ contributors, while governance remains on-chain. Uniswap Foundation, Lido Foundation, and Ethereum Foundation all use variations of this model. Switzerland's "Stiftung" (foundation) is another popular option, particularly because Swiss law is favorable to crypto organizations and the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) has provided clear guidance on token classifications.

The Marshall Islands DAO LLC

In 2022, the Republic of the Marshall Islands passed the DAO Act, recognizing DAOs as legal entities with features specifically designed for decentralized governance. The law explicitly allows DAOs to be governed entirely by smart contracts and does not require a physical office or local directors. MIDAO (Marshall Islands DAO) has assisted several projects in registering under this framework, making it one of the most DAO-friendly jurisdictions globally.

DAOs and Indian Law

Indian Regulatory Context

India does not yet have a specific legal framework for DAOs. However, several existing structures can serve as partial legal wrappers:

1. Multi-State Cooperative Society (MSCS Act, 2002): Indian cooperative societies share philosophical roots with DAOs - member-owned, democratic governance. A DAO focused on Indian users could potentially register as a cooperative society, where the cooperative's bylaws reference the on-chain governance mechanism. The MSCS Act allows societies operating across state boundaries, which mirrors a DAO's borderless nature. India has a long tradition of cooperatives - from Amul (Gujarat Cooperative Milk Marketing Federation) to IFFCO - making this a culturally resonant structure.

2. Section 8 Company (Companies Act, 2013): A non-profit company limited by guarantee could serve as a legal wrapper for a DAO focused on public goods. The company's articles of association could incorporate references to on-chain voting, though enforcement of smart contract decisions through Indian courts remains untested.

3. Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): For smaller DAOs with identified members, an LLP provides liability protection and tax transparency. However, the LLP structure requires designated partners, which conflicts with the permissionless nature of most DAOs.

Regulatory considerations: The Reserve Bank of India and SEBI have not issued specific guidance on DAO governance tokens. If a governance token is classified as a "security" under Indian law, the DAO would need to comply with SEBI's securities regulations, including KYC and accredited investor requirements. The 2022 crypto tax regime (30% flat tax on virtual digital asset gains, 1% TDS on transfers above the threshold) applies to governance token transactions, which could impact participation economics in Indian-focused DAOs. Builders should monitor regulatory developments from MeitY (Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology) and RBI circulars for any future DAO-specific guidance.

08A   Building a DAO on MST

Step-by-Step - Designing a Governance Token and DAO Architecture on MST

Building a DAO on MST takes advantage of sub-paisa transaction costs, fast finality, and the hybrid permissioned-permissionless architecture. This section walks through the key design decisions you will face when structuring a DAO on MST, from token economics to governance parameters and treasury architecture.

Step 1: Governance Token Design

Your governance token is the foundation of your DAO. On MST, you deploy an ERC-20 token with the ERC-20Votes extension (for delegation and vote checkpointing). Key design decisions include:

A
Fixed supply vs. inflationary
A fixed supply (e.g., 100 million tokens) creates scarcity but can concentrate power among early holders over time. An inflationary model (e.g., 2% annual issuance distributed as staking rewards) dilutes inactive holders and incentivizes ongoing participation. MakerDAO uses a fixed supply with occasional minting and burning; Optimism's OP token has a planned inflation schedule. For an MST-based DAO targeting Indian retail users, a modest inflation schedule that rewards active voters can help maintain healthy participation rates.
B
Distribution strategy
How you distribute the token determines who governs your DAO from day one. Common approaches include: airdrop to early users (Uniswap-style), retroactive public goods funding (Optimism), liquidity mining (Compound), or a community sale. On MST, the low transaction costs make airdrops to hundreds of thousands of wallets economically feasible - a single airdrop to 500,000 addresses would cost under Rs 3,000 in gas on MST versus over Rs 5 crore on Ethereum mainnet. This opens the door to mass-distribution strategies that were previously impossible.
C
Vesting and lockups
Team and investor tokens should vest over 2-4 years with a cliff period (typically 6-12 months). This prevents early dumping and aligns long-term incentives. The vesting contract on MST can include automatic delegation, so that vesting tokens still participate in governance even before they are unlocked - a feature that is too gas-expensive to implement on Ethereum but practical on MST.

Step 2: Governance Parameters

With sub-paisa voting costs on MST, you can tune your DAO's governance parameters more aggressively than on Ethereum:

1
Lower proposal threshold
On Ethereum, the proposal threshold for Uniswap is 2.5 million UNI tokens (worth millions of dollars) to prevent spam proposals that waste gas. On MST, you can set the threshold as low as 1,000 tokens, enabling grassroots proposals from small holders without worrying about gas costs for voters evaluating and voting on those proposals.
2
Shorter voting periods
Because voting is practically free on MST, you can shorten the voting period from 7 days (common on Ethereum) to 3 days or even 48 hours for routine operational decisions. Urgent proposals can have even shorter windows with higher quorum requirements to compensate for the reduced deliberation time. This allows MST DAOs to respond to market conditions and opportunities far more quickly than their Ethereum counterparts.
3
On-chain temperature checks
On Ethereum, DAOs use off-chain tools like Snapshot for preliminary polling because on-chain voting is expensive. On MST, even temperature checks and informal polls can be fully on-chain, creating a transparent and immutable record of community sentiment at every stage of decision-making. This eliminates the trust assumption required by off-chain voting tools and provides a single source of truth for the DAO's deliberation history.

Step 3: Treasury Architecture

A DAO on MST can use a tiered treasury structure: a cold treasury (large multisig with a high signing threshold, e.g., 5-of-9) for long-term reserves, and a hot treasury (governed directly by the Governor contract) for routine payouts and grants. The near-zero transaction costs on MST make it practical to split treasury operations across multiple contracts without incurring prohibitive gas overhead. Contributors can be paid weekly or even daily through streaming payment contracts - something impractical on Ethereum due to the gas cost of each payment transaction.

MST advantage: On Ethereum, a DAO paying 50 contributors monthly spends over Rs 25,000 in gas fees just for salary disbursement. On MST, the same 50 transactions cost less than Rs 1 total. This frees up treasury funds for actual productive work instead of network fees - a critical advantage for bootstrapped DAOs operating with limited resources.
09   Common Pitfalls

Don't Let Your DAO Become a Tyranny

⚠️
Plutocracy - rule by the rich
1 token = 1 vote can lead to a few whales controlling all decisions. Mitigation: quadratic voting, delegation, reputation-based voting, or capped voting power.
⚠️
Voter apathy
Most token holders don't vote. Typical participation is 5 - 15%. This can lead to a small group capturing the DAO. Incentives (like staking rewards for voting) and delegation are crucial.
⚠️
Slow decision-making
A proposal can take a week from submission to execution. In a fast-moving market, this can be fatal. Some DAOs have "emergency multisig" for urgent actions, but that centralizes power.

10A   Deep Dive Case Study

The DAO Hack of 2016 - A $60 Million Lesson in Smart Contract Security

"The DAO" was not just any DAO - it was the DAO, the first major experiment in decentralized venture capital on Ethereum. Launched in April 2016, it raised 12.7 million ETH (approximately $150 million at the time) from over 11,000 investors in what was then the largest crowdfunding event in history. The concept was revolutionary: token holders would vote on which projects to fund, and profits would flow back to the DAO. It held nearly 14% of all Ether in circulation. Then, on June 17, 2016, everything went wrong.

What Happened: The Reentrancy Attack

The DAO's smart contract had a function called splitDAO() that allowed dissatisfied members to withdraw their funds into a "child DAO." The vulnerability lay in the order of operations within this function. The contract sent ETH to the caller before updating the caller's balance in its internal ledger. An attacker exploited this by writing a malicious contract with a fallback() function that, upon receiving ETH, immediately called splitDAO() again - before the first call had finished executing.

This is a reentrancy attack. The attacker's contract re-entered the withdrawal function repeatedly, each time receiving ETH because the balance had not yet been decremented. The sequence looked like this:

FIGURE 15.1-B - The Reentrancy Attack on The DAO
  Attacker calls splitDAO()
    |
    +---> DAO checks balance: sufficient
    |
    +---> DAO sends ETH to attacker's contract
    |       |
    |       +---> Attacker's fallback() function triggers
    |               |
    |               +---> Calls splitDAO() AGAIN (re-enters)
    |                       |
    |                       +---> DAO checks balance: STILL sufficient (not updated yet!)
    |                       |
    |                       +---> DAO sends ETH again
    |                       |       |
    |                       |       +---> Calls splitDAO() AGAIN...
    |                       |               (repeats until gas runs out)
    |                       |
    |                       +---> Balance finally updated (too late)
    |
    +---> Original balance update (too late - ETH already drained)
The attacker re-entered the withdrawal function repeatedly before the contract could update the balance, draining approximately 3.6 million ETH ($60 million at the time).

The Vulnerable Code Pattern

The vulnerability was a classic "check-effects-interactions" violation. The contract interacted with an external address (sending ETH) before applying the effect (updating the balance). The secure pattern reverses this order: first update the balance (effect), then send the ETH (interaction). Here is the difference in pseudocode:

// VULNERABLE pattern (what The DAO did):
function withdraw(uint amount) {
    require(balances[msg.sender] >= amount);
    msg.sender.call{value: amount}("");  // Sends ETH FIRST
    balances[msg.sender] -= amount;       // Updates balance AFTER (too late!)
}

// SECURE pattern (checks-effects-interactions):
function withdraw(uint amount) {
    require(balances[msg.sender] >= amount);  // CHECK
    balances[msg.sender] -= amount;           // EFFECT (update state first)
    msg.sender.call{value: amount}("");       // INTERACTION (send ETH last)
}

The Aftermath: Ethereum's Most Controversial Hard Fork

The attack drained 3.6 million ETH into a child DAO controlled by the attacker. Fortunately, due to The DAO's built-in timelock (a 28-day waiting period before funds could be withdrawn from a child DAO), the Ethereum community had time to respond. After weeks of intense debate across forums, social media, and developer calls, the community voted to execute a hard fork - a change to Ethereum's protocol that effectively reversed the hack by moving The DAO's funds to a recovery contract where investors could reclaim their ETH.

This decision was deeply controversial. Many argued that "code is law" and that reversing a transaction, even a malicious one, violated the fundamental principle of blockchain immutability. If the community could reverse one hack, what would stop them from reversing others? Those who opposed the fork continued running the original unforked chain, which became Ethereum Classic (ETC). The split persists to this day - a permanent reminder of the philosophical divide between pragmatism and immutability absolutism.

Indian Context

The DAO hack debate is analogous to a situation in Indian banking. When a bank's internal software has a bug that allows a hacker to withdraw crores, the RBI can order the bank to reverse the transactions. But in blockchain, there is no RBI. The community itself must decide whether to "reverse" the hack or let the code stand as written. The Ethereum fork was, in effect, the community acting as its own regulator - a precedent that continues to shape how blockchain communities think about governance, immutability, and intervention.

Lessons for Modern DAOs and Smart Contract Security

1
Always follow the checks-effects-interactions pattern
Update contract state before making external calls. This single rule would have prevented The DAO hack entirely. Modern Solidity compilers and static analysis tools like Slither and Mythril automatically flag violations of this pattern during development.
2
Use reentrancy guards
OpenZeppelin's ReentrancyGuard modifier uses a simple boolean lock to prevent a function from being called again before the first invocation completes. Adding nonReentrant to any function that sends ETH or calls external contracts is now considered mandatory in professional Solidity development.
3
Professional security audits are non-negotiable
The DAO's code was reviewed informally but never formally audited by a professional security firm. Today, reputable DAOs undergo multiple independent audits from firms like Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin Security, and Consensys Diligence, and run bug bounty programs on platforms like Immunefi offering up to $10 million for critical vulnerability disclosures.
4
Use battle-tested libraries instead of custom code
Instead of writing governance and financial contracts from scratch, modern DAOs use OpenZeppelin's Governor framework and related contracts, which have been audited multiple times and are deployed by hundreds of production DAOs. The DAO hack directly motivated the creation of these standardized, rigorously tested building blocks that the entire ecosystem relies on today.

12   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 15.1

DAO
Decentralized Autonomous Organization - an entity governed by smart contracts and token-holder voting, with no central authority.
Governance Token
An ERC-20 token that grants voting rights in a DAO, often also representing economic ownership or utility.
Quorum
The minimum number of votes (or percentage of total supply) required for a proposal to be valid and pass.
Timelock
A smart contract that delays the execution of a passed proposal, giving stakeholders time to react or exit before changes take effect.
Liquid Democracy
A governance model where token holders can either vote directly on issues or delegate their voting power to representatives.
Plutocracy
Rule by the wealthy - a risk in token-voting systems where large holders can dominate decisions.
Rage Quit
A mechanism (pioneered by Moloch DAO) that allows dissenting members to burn their governance tokens and withdraw their proportional share of the treasury before a passed proposal executes.
Snapshot Voting
An off-chain, gasless voting system where token holders sign messages with their wallets to cast votes. Results are stored on IPFS and are typically non-binding, used for temperature checks before formal on-chain proposals.
Multisig
A multi-signature wallet that requires M-of-N authorized signers to approve a transaction (e.g., 3-of-5). Used by DAOs to secure treasury funds and execute high-value operations with distributed trust.
Governor Contract
The core smart contract of a DAO that manages the proposal lifecycle - creation, voting, queuing, and execution. OpenZeppelin's Governor is the most widely used implementation on EVM-compatible chains.
Proposal Threshold
The minimum number of governance tokens a member must hold (or have delegated to them) to submit a new proposal. This prevents spam proposals and ensures proposers have meaningful stake in the DAO's outcome.
Quadratic Voting
A voting mechanism where the cost of each additional vote increases quadratically (1 vote = 1 token, 2 votes = 4 tokens, 3 votes = 9 tokens), reducing the influence of wealthy participants and favoring broad community support.
