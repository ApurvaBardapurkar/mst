02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Two Founders Who Built a Solution Nobody Wanted

📖 Story

In 2022, two passionate developers spent six months building a blockchain-based voting system for student elections. The smart contracts were elegant, the UI was slick, and the gas costs were optimized. They launched on a major EVM chain. Total users after three months: 12. The student council didn't care about immutability; the existing Google Form was good enough. The project failed - not because of technology, but because nobody needed it.


Contrast that with a team that spent a week researching the Indian gig economy. They interviewed delivery riders and discovered a trust gap: riders didn't trust the platform's rating system, and customers feared fake reviews. They built a decentralized reputation system where every rating is on-chain, tamper-proof, and portable between platforms. They launched a pilot with 50 riders. Within months, a major logistics company offered to integrate it. The difference: one team started with the technology; the other started with the problem.

This sub-module ensures you start with the problem. You'll learn to look at the world through a "blockchain lens" - not to force the technology onto every situation, but to recognize the rare, high-impact opportunities where it truly belongs.

03   Background & History

The Art of Ideation - How Great Web3 Products Are Born

Blockchain ideation has evolved rapidly. Early projects (2013 - 2016) focused almost exclusively on digital currency and simple tokens. The mindset was "how can we copy Bitcoin but slightly different?" By 2017, the ICO boom shifted ideation to "what token can we sell?" - often with little real-world utility. This era produced many whitepapers and few working products.

The DeFi summer of 2020 marked a turning point. Uniswap, Aave, and Compound proved that blockchain could solve genuine financial problems - permissionless access, transparency, and composability. Suddenly, ideation became user-centered. The question changed from "what can we build on Ethereum?" to "what problems do people have that only blockchain can solve?"

Today, with chains like MST offering high throughput, sub-paisa fees, and easy user onboarding, the ideation canvas has expanded further. Problems that were impractical on Ethereum - micro-payments for content, real-time gaming assets, low-cost supply chain tracking - are now feasible. The best capstone projects sit at this intersection: a real human need, plus a blockchain feature that traditional infrastructure cannot provide.

Golden rule of Web3 ideation: "Don't look for a problem for your blockchain. Look for a problem that only a blockchain can solve." If a centralized database can do it cheaper and faster, let it.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Ideation Funnel - From Fog to Focus

🔍 The Analogy

Ideation is like panning for gold. You start with a broad river - every industry, every pain point, every inefficiency. You scoop up promising sand (ideas) and run it through increasingly fine filters: "Is there real demand?", "Can a blockchain fix this?", "Can I build it in a few weeks?" Most ideas wash away. The nuggets that remain are your capstone candidates.

The ideation funnel has four stages. Click each to explore:

①
DIVERGE
Generate as many ideas as possible
Think about industries you know: education, healthcare, logistics, entertainment. List problems. Don't judge yet. At this stage, quantity beats quality. Examples: "freelancers don't get paid on time", "farmers can't prove organic certification", "concert tickets get scalped".
②
FILTER
Apply the blockchain litmus test
For each idea, ask five questions: 1) Is there a trust gap between parties? 2) Does the problem involve multiple entities that don't share a database? 3) Is there a need for permanent, auditable records? 4) Can smart contracts automate a manual process? 5) Would decentralization reduce costs or increase participation? Ideas scoring 3+ "yes" answers survive to the next stage.
③
VALIDATE
Talk to real users, check feasibility
Find 5 potential users (friends, online communities, professionals) and describe your idea in one sentence. Listen to their reactions. Do they say "I need this" or "that's interesting"? Check if a similar dApp already exists. Research the legal landscape (e.g., financial services require licenses).
④
SELECT
Choose your capstone and commit
Pick the idea with the best balance of impact, feasibility, and personal interest. You'll be working on this for weeks; passion matters. Write a one-page proposal. Share it with peers for feedback. Then lock in your choice and move to architecture design (9.2).

05   The Blockchain Litmus Test

Does Your Idea Actually Need a Blockchain?

This is the most important question you'll ask during ideation. Many promising-sounding ideas fail this test because a centralized database or simple web app would work just as well. Here's the detailed checklist, with examples:

Criteria✅ Blockchain-Worthy❌ Better Without Blockchain
Trust gapMultiple parties transact but don't trust each other (e.g., buyer and seller on a freelance platform)All users trust a central authority (e.g., internal company tool)
Multiple writersMany independent entities need to write to a shared record (e.g., hospitals updating a patient's medical history)Only one entity writes; others only read (e.g., a blog)
Immutability requirementRecords must be tamper-proof and auditable forever (e.g., land titles, academic credentials)Data can be edited or deleted by an admin (e.g., user preferences)
Disintermediation gainRemoving a middleman significantly reduces cost or increases speed (e.g., remittances without banks)The intermediary adds real value that can't be automated (e.g., doctors diagnosing patients)
Automation potentialA smart contract can automate a manual, error-prone process (e.g., insurance claims triggered by weather data)The process requires human judgment in every case (e.g., creative design)

Let's apply this test to two ideas:

✅
Decentralized Freelance Platform (Passes)
Trust gap: clients and freelancers don't trust each other. Multiple writers: both parties update order status. Immutability: dispute records must be auditable. Disintermediation: removes 20% platform fee. Automation: escrow releases automatically upon confirmation. Verdict: Strong blockchain fit.
❌
To-Do List App (Fails)
Only one writer (the user). No trust gap. No disintermediation. A centralized database is faster, cheaper, and simpler. Verdict: Not a blockchain use case.

Applying the Litmus Test to Indian-Specific Problems

Now let's apply the same litmus test to four problems deeply rooted in the Indian context. These examples will help you think about blockchain suitability for the kinds of challenges you encounter across India's diverse economic landscape - from rural employment schemes to urban tax systems.

✅
MNREGA Wage Payment Tracking (Passes - 5/5)
Background: The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MNREGA) guarantees 100 days of wage employment per year to rural households. However, reports consistently reveal delayed payments, ghost workers on muster rolls, and middlemen siphoning funds. In 2023 alone, multiple RTI queries exposed discrepancies amounting to crores of rupees across states.

Trust gap: Workers don't trust that their attendance is recorded accurately. Gram Panchayats, Block Development Officers, and banks each maintain separate records that often conflict.
Multiple writers: At least four entities - the worker (biometric attendance), the mate (supervisor), the Panchayat office, and the paying bank - all update the same employment record.
Immutability: Once a worker's attendance is recorded, it should never be altered retroactively, as this is the primary vector for fraud.
Disintermediation: Smart contracts could automatically trigger wage transfers to the worker's bank account once attendance is confirmed, removing delays caused by manual file movement across offices.
Automation: A smart contract could enforce the rule "if attendance >= 14 days this fortnight, release payment within 48 hours" without any human approval step.
Verdict: Excellent blockchain fit. MST's sub-paisa fees make it feasible to record each daily attendance as an on-chain transaction, even for millions of workers.
✅
GST Invoice Verification (Passes - 4/5)
Background: India's Goods and Services Tax system processes billions of invoices annually. A persistent problem is "fake invoicing" - businesses create fictitious invoices to claim fraudulent Input Tax Credit (ITC). The GST Council has identified this as a multi-thousand-crore leakage. Currently, cross-verification between GSTR-1 (sales) and GSTR-2A (purchases) relies on centralized matching with significant delays.

Trust gap: The buyer, seller, and tax authority each have different incentives. Sellers may inflate invoices; buyers may claim credits for goods never received.
Multiple writers: Every business in the supply chain writes invoice data - manufacturer, wholesaler, retailer, and transporter.
Immutability: Once an invoice is issued and accepted by both parties, it must be permanent and auditable for at least 7 years (as per tax law).
Disintermediation: Partial - the tax authority still needs to exist, but the verification process can be automated end-to-end.
Automation: A smart contract can automatically flag mismatches in real time rather than months later during annual reconciliation.
Verdict: Strong blockchain fit. Hybrid zones on MST could keep sensitive invoice amounts in permissioned lanes while allowing public verification of invoice existence and matching status.
✅
Microfinance Lending Circles - SHG Ledger (Passes - 4/5)
Background: Self-Help Groups (SHGs) are the backbone of India's microfinance sector. Over 80 lakh SHGs manage internal lending - members pool savings and give small loans to each other. The problem: record-keeping is manual (paper registers), disputes about contributions are common, and when an SHG leader defaults or migrates, the records often vanish. Banks lending to SHGs struggle to verify repayment histories.

Trust gap: Individual members don't fully trust the group treasurer. The lending bank doesn't trust the SHG's paper records. Members who have repaid loans have no verifiable proof of creditworthiness.
Multiple writers: Each member contributes savings, borrows, and repays - all 10 to 20 members are active participants writing to the shared ledger.
Immutability: Contribution and repayment records must be permanent so that no single member can dispute their history or the treasurer cannot alter records after the fact.
Disintermediation: Moderate - the SHG facilitator (often an NGO) could be partially replaced by smart contract rules for loan disbursement and repayment tracking.
Automation: Smart contracts can enforce repayment schedules, calculate interest automatically, and maintain transparent ledgers accessible to all members via a simple mobile interface.
Verdict: Strong blockchain fit. SARAL onboarding is critical here - SHG members in rural India are unlikely to manage wallets manually. Google login or Aadhaar-linked onboarding makes this practical. The portable credit history each member builds on-chain becomes a valuable asset when they approach formal banks for larger loans.
✅
Agricultural Supply Chain - Mandi to Market (Passes - 5/5)
Background: Indian farmers sell produce through a chain of intermediaries - village aggregators, APMC mandi agents (commission agents or "arthiyas"), wholesalers, and finally retailers. At each step, the price markup is significant, yet the farmer receives only 15-25% of the final consumer price. Worse, there is no way for a consumer to verify claims like "organic", "pesticide-free", or "farm-fresh" - these labels are completely unauditable in the current system.

Trust gap: Farmers don't trust that mandi prices are fair. Consumers don't trust organic labels. Intermediaries don't trust each other's quality assessments.
Multiple writers: Farmer, aggregator, transporter, mandi agent, wholesaler, retailer - at least six entities touch the produce and need to record handovers.
Immutability: Once a farmer's produce is graded and priced at the mandi, that record should be permanent - preventing post-facto price manipulation that currently costs farmers lakhs annually.
Disintermediation: Removing even one intermediary could increase farmer income by 10-30%. A direct farmer-to-retailer channel on-chain could be genuinely transformative for rural livelihoods.
Automation: Smart contracts can automate payments at each handover point and release funds to the farmer within minutes of delivery confirmation, rather than the current 3 to 15 day settlement cycle.
Verdict: Excellent blockchain fit. WASMify can verify IoT data from cold-chain sensors and GPS trackers, submitting zero-knowledge proofs to the chain. MST's low fees make it practical to record every handover for even a Rs. 50 bag of vegetables.
🔍 Pattern Recognition

Notice a pattern in the Indian examples above? The strongest blockchain use cases share three traits: (1) multiple independent parties who don't share a database, (2) significant financial stakes where manipulation is tempting, and (3) a paper-based or fragmented digital system that creates information asymmetry. When you spot all three in an industry, you've likely found a problem worth solving on-chain. India, with its vast informal economy, complex regulatory landscape, and rapidly growing digital infrastructure, is fertile ground for exactly these kinds of opportunities.

🗝️ The Acid Test

If you can remove the blockchain from your idea and nothing breaks, you don't need a blockchain. The best capstone ideas are the ones where the blockchain isn't a feature - it's the foundation.

06   Interactive Ideation Workshop

Find Your Capstone Idea - Step by Step

Work through these exercises. By the end, you'll have a shortlist of validated ideas.

📝 Exercise 1: The Pain Point Harvest

Think about industries you've personally experienced: education, healthcare, freelancing, gaming, social media, e-commerce, logistics, banking. For each, ask: "What frustrated me as a user? What felt unfair? Where did I have to trust a company that I didn't trust?" Write down 10 pain points. Don't censor yourself.

Examples: "UPI payments sometimes fail and I have to trust the bank's dispute resolution", "My university degree can be faked easily", "Online game items I bought disappeared when the server shut down".

🧪 Exercise 2: Apply the Litmus Test

Take your 10 pain points and score each on the five criteria (1 = not at all, 5 = extremely). Sum the scores. Ideas scoring 18+ are strong candidates. Ideas scoring below 12 probably aren't blockchain problems.

🗣️ Exercise 3: The 5-Minute Validation

Take your top 3 ideas. For each, find one person in your network who works in that industry or has experienced that pain point. Describe your idea in 60 seconds. Ask: "Would you use this? What would make you not use it?" Write down their exact words. This is gold.

📊 Exercise 4: The MST Fit Check

For your top idea, ask: "Which MST feature makes this possible or better?" Is it the sub-paisa fees (micropayments, frequent transactions)? SARAL (onboarding non-crypto users)? WASMify (verifiable off-chain data)? Hybrid zones (permissioned + public)? Be specific. If your idea could run identically on Ethereum, you're not leveraging MST's advantage.

📖 Example Proposal - KisanChain

Project Title: KisanChain - Transparent Farm-to-Fork Tracking

Problem Statement: Indian farmers selling through APMC mandis receive only 15-25% of the final retail price due to a chain of intermediaries. Consumers have no way to verify "organic" or "pesticide-free" claims. Over 3 crore small and marginal farmers are affected. Current solutions - the government e-NAM portal and private agri-tech apps - still rely on centralized databases that intermediaries can manipulate. There is no immutable audit trail from farm to plate.

Blockchain Justification: This problem scores 5/5 on the litmus test. There is a severe trust gap between farmers, intermediaries, and consumers. Six independent parties write to the supply chain record. Produce grading and pricing must be immutable to prevent post-facto manipulation. Removing even one intermediary increases farmer income by 10-30%. Smart contracts can automate payment release upon delivery confirmation.

Solution Overview: KisanChain is a dApp where farmers register their produce with a QR code at the point of harvest. At each handover - aggregator, transporter, mandi, wholesaler, retailer - the QR code is scanned and the transaction is recorded on MST. Consumers scan the QR code on the retail package to see the full journey: who grew it, when it was harvested, how it was transported, and what price the farmer received. Payments flow through an escrow smart contract that releases funds to the farmer within minutes of delivery confirmation.

MST Feature Mapping: Sub-paisa fees make it practical to record every handover, even for low-value produce (a Rs. 50 bag of tomatoes). WASMify verifies temperature data from cold-chain IoT sensors and submits ZK proofs on-chain. SARAL enables farmers to onboard using their phone number without managing a wallet or understanding cryptographic key pairs.

Target Users: Small and marginal farmers (land holding under 2 hectares) in Maharashtra and Karnataka who sell through APMC mandis. Secondary users are urban consumers in Pune and Bangalore who actively seek organic and traceable food products and are willing to pay a small premium for verified provenance.

Core Features (MVP):

  • Farmer registration and produce listing with QR code generation
  • Supply chain handover recording (scan QR, update status, record on-chain)
  • Consumer-facing product journey viewer (scan QR to see full history)
  • Escrow smart contract for automated farmer payments
  • Simple dashboard showing farmer earnings vs. retail price transparency

Success Metrics: (1) At least 20 farmers onboarded in the pilot phase. (2) At least 100 produce items tracked end-to-end from farm to retail. (3) Average farmer payment time reduced from 7 days to under 1 hour.

Competitive Landscape: Existing solutions like e-NAM (government) and Ninjacart (private) use centralized databases and do not provide consumers with verifiable provenance. AgriLedger (UK-based) uses blockchain but operates on Ethereum with high gas costs, making per-item tracking impractical for low-value Indian produce. KisanChain differentiates by combining MST's sub-paisa fees with SARAL onboarding for tech-unfamiliar farmers, making it the first solution that is both economically viable and accessible to the rural Indian user base.

Pro tip: Write your proposal as if you are explaining the project to a smart friend who knows nothing about blockchain. If they can understand the problem and why blockchain helps, you have nailed it. Jargon-heavy proposals are usually hiding weak thinking beneath impressive-sounding terminology.
08   Real-World Case Studies

Ideation That Changed the World

🦄
Uniswap - The Simple Idea That Created a $5B Protocol
Hayden Adams was laid off from Siemens and decided to learn Ethereum. He was inspired by a Reddit post describing an automated market maker. The idea was simple: "What if anyone could trade tokens without an exchange, just by providing liquidity to a pool?" He built a proof of concept in a few weeks. Today, Uniswap is the largest DEX in the world. The idea succeeded because it solved a real problem (centralized exchange hacks, KYC barriers, listing fees) with an elegant on-chain solution. Your capstone doesn't need to be Uniswap - but it should solve a problem as clearly as Uniswap solved token trading.
🎓
IIT Madras - Blockchain Credentials on a Budget
In 2022, IIT Madras started issuing degree certificates as NFTs on a blockchain. The problem was real: fake degrees are a massive industry in India. The solution was simple: issue each degree as an ERC-721 token with metadata linking to the student's identity. Employers can verify a degree in seconds by checking the blockchain. The cost per certificate? A few rupees. This is exactly the kind of high-impact, low-cost project that shines on MST, where minting an NFT costs ₹0.02.
🇮🇳
Polygon (Matic Network) - From Mumbai Apartment to Global L2
Polygon was co-founded in 2017 by Jaynti Kanani, Sandeep Nailwal, and Anurag Arjun - three Indian developers who saw a specific, painful problem: Ethereum was too slow and expensive for mainstream adoption. Their ideation started not with "let's build a blockchain" but with a simple user frustration: "Why can't my friend use a dApp without paying $15 in gas fees?" They built a Layer 2 scaling solution that processes thousands of transactions per second at a fraction of the cost. Starting from a small apartment in Mumbai, they grew Polygon into a protocol partnering with Starbucks, Reddit, Disney, and the Indian state of Maharashtra for land record digitization. By 2023, Polygon had processed over 2 billion transactions. The lesson for your capstone: Polygon's founders didn't invent a new technology from scratch. They solved a practical bottleneck that was preventing real users from accessing existing technology. Your capstone can follow the same principle - find the bottleneck, design a solution, remove the friction.
💰
India's CBDC - The Digital Rupee Pilot
In December 2022, the Reserve Bank of India launched the e-Rupee (Digital Rupee) pilot in select cities - Mumbai, New Delhi, Bangalore, and Bhubaneswar. The retail CBDC pilot included major banks like SBI, ICICI, Yes Bank, and IDFC First Bank. The problem being addressed was substantial: cash management costs India an estimated Rs. 21,000 crore annually for printing, distributing, securing, and destroying old notes. Additionally, cross-border remittances - crucial for India as the world's largest recipient of remittances at $100 billion+ annually - are slow and expensive through traditional banking channels.

The Digital Rupee uses a distributed ledger but is not fully decentralized - the RBI remains the central authority. This is an important lesson for your ideation: not every blockchain project needs to be fully decentralized. Permissioned or hybrid models (like MST's hybrid zones) can solve real problems while satisfying regulatory requirements. The Digital Rupee pilot demonstrates that even the most conservative financial institution in India recognizes the value of distributed ledger technology for specific use cases. When ideating, consider where a hybrid approach might be more practical than full decentralization.
📱
CoinSwitch Kuber - Simplifying Crypto Access for Bharat
CoinSwitch, founded by Ashish Singhal in Bangalore, became India's largest crypto investment platform with over 1.9 crore users by 2022. Their key insight during ideation was not technical but experiential: "Why does buying crypto feel like rocket science?" While global exchanges like Binance catered to traders with complex order books and advanced charting tools, CoinSwitch built an interface that looked and felt like a simple UPI payment - pick a coin, enter an amount, tap buy. They also localized deeply for India: INR deposits via UPI and bank transfer, customer support in regional languages, and KYC through Aadhaar and PAN verification.

The lesson for your capstone is powerful: user experience is often more important than technical sophistication. A dApp with a simple, familiar interface will outperform a technically superior dApp that requires a manual to use. When ideating, always ask: "Can my target user - someone who is not a crypto expert - figure this out in 30 seconds?" If not, simplify. MST's SARAL onboarding was designed with exactly this philosophy - abstracting away wallet creation, gas fees, and seed phrases so that users interact with the dApp the way they interact with any normal mobile application.
08A   Competitive Analysis for Your Idea

Research, Differentiate, and Position Your dApp

No idea exists in a vacuum. Before committing to your capstone, you must understand the competitive landscape - what already exists, why it succeeds or fails, and how your solution is different. Skipping this step is one of the most common reasons capstone projects feel unoriginal or lack a clear value proposition. A thorough competitive analysis also strengthens your proposal by showing evaluators that you understand the market and have a defensible position.

Step-by-Step Competitive Research Process

①
SEARCH
Identify existing solutions - both Web2 and Web3
Search Google, Product Hunt, DappRadar, and GitHub for projects solving the same or a similar problem. Don't limit yourself to blockchain projects - include traditional apps, government portals, and even manual processes. For example, if your idea is a decentralized freelance platform, your competitors include Upwork (Web2), Braintrust (Web3), and even WhatsApp groups where freelancers find work informally. In the Indian context, also check government portals like UMANG, DigiLocker, and e-NAM, which may already address parts of your problem using centralized infrastructure.
②
ANALYZE
Evaluate each competitor's strengths and weaknesses
For each competitor, answer these questions: What do they do well? What complaints do users have (check app store reviews, Reddit, Twitter)? What is their revenue model? Are they centralized or decentralized? What technology stack do they use? How many active users do they have? Understanding competitor weaknesses reveals the gaps your project can fill. Pay special attention to user complaints - phrases like "slow", "opaque", "unfair fees", and "no accountability" often signal problems that blockchain can solve.
③
DIFFERENTIATE
Define your unique value proposition
Your dApp must do at least one thing that no existing solution does. This could be: lower cost (thanks to MST's sub-paisa fees), trustless operation (no central authority that can censor or manipulate), data portability (users own their data and can move between platforms), or India-specific localization (UPI integration, regional language support, Aadhaar-linked onboarding). Write a single sentence: "Unlike [competitor], [your dApp] does [unique thing] because [blockchain reason]." If you can't complete that sentence convincingly, your idea may need refinement.
④
POSITION
Create a competitive positioning map
Plot competitors on a 2x2 matrix. Choose two axes that matter for your problem - for example, "cost to user" (low to high) vs. "transparency" (opaque to fully transparent). Position each competitor and your project on this map. Your dApp should occupy a unique quadrant - ideally the one that represents "low cost + high transparency" or whichever combination your target users value most. This visual tool is also excellent for including in your proposal to demonstrate strategic thinking.
🗝️ The Competitive Moat Question

Ask yourself: "If my capstone succeeds, what stops a well-funded company from copying it in a month?" In Web3, common moats include network effects (more users make the platform more valuable), data immutability (once records are on-chain, they cannot be replicated without user consent), community governance (token holders steer the protocol's direction), and composability (other dApps build on top of yours, creating ecosystem lock-in). Your proposal should mention at least one moat, even if your MVP is small. Thinking about defensibility early separates a capstone from a class project.

Remember: Your biggest competitor is often not another dApp - it is the status quo. People default to what they already know, even if it is inefficient. Your proposal must explain not just why your solution is better, but why it is better enough for users to switch from their current behavior. This is called the "switching cost barrier," and overcoming it is the difference between a project that gets users and one that collects dust.
09   Common Pitfalls in Ideation

Don't Fall Into These Traps

⚠️
"Blockchain for everything" syndrome
Forcing blockchain into a problem that doesn't need it. If your idea can be a Google Sheet, make it a Google Sheet. Only use blockchain when trustlessness, immutability, or decentralized consensus are essential.
⚠️
Ignoring the user onboarding challenge
A dApp that requires MetaMask, ETH, and seed phrases will have near-zero adoption among normal users. Leverage SARAL or other wallet abstraction solutions to make onboarding as easy as signing into Google.
⚠️
Choosing a project that's too large
"Decentralized Amazon" is a multi-year, multi-team project. Your capstone should be a focused MVP you can complete in weeks. Start with one core feature. You can always expand later.
10   Assessment Preparation

Top Facts - Know These for Your Proposal

01
A strong proposal starts with a clear problem statement
Who is affected? How severe is the problem? Why hasn't it been solved yet? The problem statement is the most important part of your proposal.
02
Explicitly justify why blockchain is necessary
Mention the trust gap, the multiple parties, the immutability requirement. Show that you applied the litmus test.
03
Connect your idea to MST's specific strengths
Name the MST feature that enables your idea (sub-paisa fees, SARAL, WASMify, hybrid zones). Show why this project works on MST but not on Ethereum.
04
Define success metrics
How will you know if your MVP is successful? User sign-ups? Transactions processed? Funds saved? Define at least one measurable goal.
11   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 9.1

Ideation
The process of generating, filtering, and validating ideas for a project or product. In blockchain, ideation focuses on finding problems that benefit from decentralization.
Trust Gap
A situation where two or more parties need to transact but do not trust each other. Blockchain bridges trust gaps by replacing trust in a person or company with trust in code.
Blockchain Litmus Test
A set of questions to determine if a problem actually requires a blockchain. Evaluates trust gap, multiple writers, immutability needs, disintermediation potential, and automation potential.
MVP (Minimum Viable Product)
The simplest version of a product that can be built and tested with real users. For a capstone, the MVP should be completable in 2-3 weeks and demonstrate core functionality.
MST Fit
The degree to which a dApp leverages MST's unique features - sub-paisa fees, SARAL onboarding, WASMify, hybrid zones - to achieve something impossible or impractical on other chains.
Problem Statement
A concise description of the real-world pain point that a project addresses. Should include who is affected, how severe the problem is, and why existing solutions fall short.
Disintermediation
The removal of intermediaries (middlemen) from a process or transaction. Blockchain enables disintermediation by allowing parties to transact directly using smart contracts instead of relying on banks, brokers, or platform operators. In the Indian context, disintermediation is particularly powerful in sectors like agriculture, remittances, and freelancing where intermediary fees can consume 15-30% of the transaction value.
Token Economics (Tokenomics)
The design of a token's supply, distribution, utility, and incentive mechanisms within a blockchain ecosystem. Good tokenomics align the interests of all participants - users, developers, and validators - so that the system grows sustainably. Key considerations include total supply (fixed vs. inflationary), distribution schedule, utility within the dApp, and governance rights attached to the token.
User Story
A short, plain-language description of a feature from the perspective of an end user, typically written as: "As a [type of user], I want [action] so that [benefit]." User stories help developers stay focused on solving real user problems rather than building features nobody needs. For example: "As a farmer, I want to see my payment status in real time so that I don't have to visit the Panchayat office repeatedly."
Competitive Moat
A sustainable advantage that protects a project from being easily copied or displaced by competitors. In Web3, common moats include network effects (more users make the platform more valuable), on-chain data ownership (records cannot be replicated without user consent), community governance (token holders steer the protocol), and composability (other dApps build on top of yours, creating ecosystem lock-in).
