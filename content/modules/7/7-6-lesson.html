02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Online Rummy Scandal That Could Have Been Prevented

📖 Story

In 2023, a popular online rummy platform in India faced a massive backlash. Several high-stakes players noticed an impossible pattern: certain opponents consistently received winning cards in crucial moments, far beyond statistical probability. The platform's response? "Trust us, our shuffle is random." But there was no way to verify this claim. The platform controlled the server, the shuffle algorithm, and the game logs. Players had to either accept the operator's word or stop playing. Thousands stopped playing.


The tragedy is that the technology to prevent this already existed. Imagine instead that every card shuffle was recorded on a blockchain with a cryptographic commitment. Before a hand begins, the platform publishes a hash of the shuffled deck. After the hand, the full deck is revealed, and any player can verify that the hash matches. A zero-knowledge proof guarantees that the shuffle was a random permutation of the original 52 cards - not pre-arranged to favour any player.


This is exactly what SatvaShuffle does on MST. For slot machines, Klethesia publishes the exact Return-to-Player (RTP) percentage on-chain, with every spin outcome verifiable against a pre-committed random seed. For loot boxes, FortunaX generates verifiable random numbers using a distributed randomness beacon, proving that the rare item drop wasn't manipulated.


MST's gaming protocols solve the fundamental trust problem in online gaming. You don't trust the casino. You trust the math - and you can verify it yourself.

The online gaming industry, projected to reach $5 billion in India alone by 2025, is built on a fragile foundation of operator trust. MST's fairness protocols replace that trust with cryptographic proof, enabling a new generation of provably fair games.

03   Background & History

From "Trust Us" to "Verify Yourself" - The Evolution of Gaming Fairness

1990s - 2000s
The Black Box Era. Online casinos ran fully centralised servers. The shuffle, the RNG, the payout percentages - everything was a black box. Regulators relied on periodic audits of source code, but there was no way for a player to verify that the code running at 2 AM was the same code that was audited. Scandals were frequent and largely undetectable.
2012
Provably Fair - The First Generation. Bitcoin casinos introduced "provably fair" algorithms. The server would commit to a random seed before a game round, and the player could verify the outcome afterward. While an improvement, these systems still relied on the server generating the seed - a sophisticated operator could manipulate the seed generation itself.
2018
On-Chain Randomness - Chainlink VRF. Chainlink introduced a Verifiable Random Function (VRF) that provided on-chain randomness from a decentralised oracle network. This solved the seed manipulation problem but was expensive (high gas fees) and not natively integrated into gaming logic like shuffling or RTP calculations.
2022 - 2023
Specialised Gaming Chains. Some L1/L2 chains targeted gaming specifically, but they focused on scalability (high TPS) rather than game-specific fairness guarantees. Shuffling and RTP were left to individual game developers to implement, leading to inconsistent and often flawed implementations.
2024 - Today
MST's Gaming Protocols. MST builds fairness directly into the protocol layer. Klethesia provides on-chain RTP verification for slots. SatvaShuffle uses ZK proofs for verifiable card shuffling. FortunaX delivers a distributed randomness beacon secured by Trika POSA validators. Game developers don't need to implement fairness - they inherit it from the chain.
💡 The Protocol-Level Advantage

When fairness is built into the blockchain protocol itself, game developers cannot bypass it - even if they want to. This is fundamentally different from smart-contract-based fairness, where each developer must implement (and potentially misimplement) their own solution.

The Three Protocols - Klethesia, SatvaShuffle, and FortunaX

🎰 The Casino Manager, The Dealer, and The Lottery Machine

Klethesia is like a casino manager who publishes the slot machine's exact payout rate on a public notice board, and after every spin, posts the result and the random seed so anyone can verify it. SatvaShuffle is like a card dealer who shuffles the deck inside a glass box - you can't see the cards, but you can see the shuffle happen, and afterward you can verify the deck was standard and the shuffle was random. FortunaX is like a lottery machine that broadcasts its random number generation live, with multiple independent auditors confirming each draw.

W

Why do these protocols exist?
Because players cannot trust online gaming operators to be fair. Even licensed operators have been caught manipulating outcomes. Cryptographic proof eliminates the need for trust entirely.
W

What does each protocol guarantee?
Klethesia: Guarantees slot machine RTP matches published rates; every spin is verifiable. SatvaShuffle: Guarantees a random permutation of a standard deck; no card manipulation possible. FortunaX: Guarantees unpredictable, unbiasable random numbers for any game mechanic.
H
HOW
How do they work technically?
Klethesia: Each spin is driven by a VRF output. The RTP is enforced by a smart contract that accumulates payouts and verifies the long-term percentage matches the advertised rate. SatvaShuffle: Uses a ZK-SNARK to prove that a shuffled deck is a valid permutation of the original deck, without revealing the deck order until after the hand. FortunaX: Combines entropy from multiple validators into a single random number using threshold BLS signatures, ensuring no single validator can predict or bias the result.
W

Where are they applied?
Klethesia: Slot machines, crash games, roulette. SatvaShuffle: Rummy, poker, teen patti, any card game. FortunaX: Loot boxes, lottery draws, prize wheels, any game needing a single random outcome.

What are their limitations?
Klethesia's RTP verification is statistical - it requires a large number of spins to prove the advertised RTP with confidence. SatvaShuffle's ZK proofs add computational overhead (~1-2 seconds for proof generation, though verification is fast). FortunaX's distributed randomness requires validator participation; if too many validators are offline, randomness generation may be delayed (mitigated by a fallback threshold scheme).
05   Technical Deep Dive

Inside Klethesia, SatvaShuffle, and FortunaX

1
Klethesia - Provable RTP for Slot Machines
Klethesia enforces Return-to-Player (RTP) at the protocol level. Each slot machine is a smart contract that registers its advertised RTP (e.g., 96.5%). Every spin generates a random outcome using FortunaX's randomness beacon. The contract tracks cumulative bets and cumulative payouts. Over a configurable epoch (e.g., 100,000 spins), the contract verifies that the actual RTP falls within an acceptable statistical band (±1%) of the advertised rate. If the operator attempts to lower the effective RTP below the band, the contract can automatically freeze and refund players. Additionally, every spin outcome is published on-chain with a commitment to the random seed, allowing auditors to verify the fairness of individual results.
2
SatvaShuffle - ZK-Verified Card Shuffling
SatvaShuffle is a zero-knowledge shuffle protocol. Before a hand, the dealer (the game smart contract) generates a permutation of a 52-card deck using FortunaX randomness. It commits to the shuffled deck by publishing a Merkle root of the card positions on-chain. A ZK-SNARK proof is generated to attest: "There exists a permutation of the standard 52-card set that produces this Merkle root." This proof can be verified by any player for ~200k gas, guaranteeing the deck is a valid shuffle without revealing the card order. As the hand progresses, cards are revealed by publishing Merkle proofs from the committed root. After the hand, the full deck can be revealed, and players can independently recompute the Merkle root to confirm no cards were added, removed, or swapped.
3
FortunaX - Distributed Randomness Beacon
FortunaX is MST's native randomness source. It operates as an on-chain randomness beacon that produces unpredictable, unbiasable random values at regular intervals. The beacon uses a threshold BLS signature scheme: each Trika POSA validator contributes a random share (derived from a VRF seeded by the previous beacon output). Once a threshold of shares (e.g., 2/3) is collected, they are aggregated into a single BLS signature, which serves as the random output. Because no single validator knows the final output until the threshold is reached, and because the output is deterministic given the shares, the randomness is unpredictable (no validator can bias it without colluding with 2/3 of the set) and publicly verifiable (anyone can check the BLS signature against the known public keys). Game contracts request a random number by calling the FortunaX precompile, which returns the latest beacon output along with a proof of correctness.
🗝️ Why Protocol-Level Fairness Matters

If a game developer deploys on MST, they automatically use FortunaX for randomness, Klethesia for RTP (if applicable), and SatvaShuffle for card games. The chain enforces fairness - the developer cannot write a contract that bypasses these protocols. This is a far stronger guarantee than a "fairness audit" of a centralised game server.

06   Mathematics of Verifiable Random Functions

Step-by-Step: How FortunaX Generates Provably Random Numbers

At the heart of FortunaX lies a Verifiable Random Function (VRF) - a cryptographic primitive that produces a random output along with a proof that the output was correctly computed. Understanding the mathematics behind VRF is essential to appreciating why FortunaX's randomness cannot be manipulated.

1
Step 1: Key Generation
Each validator generates a private key (sk) and a corresponding public key (pk = sk * G), where G is the generator point on an elliptic curve. The public key is registered on-chain and known to all participants. The private key remains secret. Think of this like a validator creating a unique mathematical identity that everyone can recognise but nobody else can impersonate.
2
Step 2: Input Seeding
The VRF takes an input seed (alpha) - in FortunaX, this is the hash of the previous beacon output concatenated with the current block number. This creates a deterministic chain: the input for round N depends on the output of round N-1. The formula is: alpha_N = Hash(beacon_output_{N-1} || block_number_N). This chaining ensures that randomness cannot be "replayed" from a previous round.
3
Step 3: VRF Evaluation
The validator computes the VRF output: gamma = sk * H(alpha), where H is a hash-to-curve function that maps the input seed to a point on the elliptic curve. The random output is then: beta = Hash(gamma). The crucial property is that beta is uniquely determined by sk and alpha - there is exactly one valid output for any given input and key pair. A different private key would produce a completely different output, and the same private key with a different input also produces a completely different output.
4
Step 4: Proof Generation
Along with the output, the validator generates a proof (pi) that demonstrates gamma was correctly computed from the private key and input, without revealing the private key. This uses a Schnorr-like signature scheme on the elliptic curve. The proof consists of values (c, s) such that: s * G = pk * c + gamma and s * H(alpha) = gamma * c + some commitment. Anyone with the public key, input seed, output, and proof can verify correctness in constant time.
5
Step 5: Threshold Aggregation
In FortunaX, individual VRF outputs from multiple validators are combined using threshold BLS aggregation. If there are N validators and the threshold is t (typically 2/3 of N), then any t validators contributing their shares produces a unique aggregate signature. The final random number is: R = Hash(BLS_Aggregate(share_1, share_2, ..., share_t)). Because the aggregate is deterministic given any t shares, and because no single validator (or even t-1 colluding validators) can predict the final aggregate before the t-th share arrives, the output is both unpredictable and unbiasable.
🔢 An Indian Analogy for VRF

Think of VRF like a traditional Indian kolam (rangoli pattern). Given the same starting point and the same set of rules (your private key), you will always draw the same unique kolam (the random output). A neighbour watching you draw can verify you followed the rules (the proof), but cannot predict what your kolam will look like unless they know your exact starting point and rules. And if ten families each draw their own kolam, combining pieces from any seven of them produces a community pattern that no single family could have predicted - this is threshold aggregation.

🗝️ The Three Security Properties of VRF

Uniqueness: For any input and public key, there is exactly one valid output. A malicious validator cannot produce two different "valid" random numbers for the same round. Pseudorandomness: Without the private key, the output is computationally indistinguishable from a truly random number. No observer can predict the output. Verifiability: Given the public key, input, output, and proof, anyone can confirm the output was correctly computed - without knowing the private key. These three properties together guarantee that FortunaX randomness cannot be predicted, biased, or faked.

08   Indian Gaming Regulation & Compliance

How On-Chain Fairness Aligns with India's Regulatory Framework

India's online gaming industry operates within a complex multi-layered regulatory environment. Understanding how MST's fairness protocols intersect with Indian law is critical for any developer or operator planning to deploy gaming applications on MST.

1
The Skill vs. Chance Distinction
Indian law differentiates between "games of skill" (legal in most states) and "games of chance" (generally prohibited as gambling). The Supreme Court's landmark rulings in cases like State of Andhra Pradesh vs. K. Satyanarayana (1968) and more recently the Junglee Games case established that games where skill predominates over chance are legal. Rummy, poker, and fantasy sports fall under "skill games." MST's SatvaShuffle protocol is particularly relevant here: by proving that card distribution is purely random and unmanipulated, it demonstrates that outcomes depend entirely on player skill rather than operator intervention. This cryptographic guarantee strengthens the legal position that the game outcome depends on player decisions, not hidden manipulation.
2
State-Level Regulations and FIFS Compliance
The Federation of Indian Fantasy Sports (FIFS) and the All India Gaming Federation (AIGF) have established self-regulatory frameworks requiring member platforms to demonstrate fair play. States like Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana have attempted various levels of regulation or prohibition. The central government's IT Rules (2023 amendments) introduced a framework for Self-Regulatory Organisations (SROs) to oversee online gaming. MST's on-chain audit trail directly satisfies the transparency requirements these bodies demand. Rather than submitting periodic audit reports (which can be fabricated), operators on MST can point regulators to a permanent, tamper-proof on-chain record of every game outcome, every shuffle, and every RTP calculation. This shifts compliance from "trust our reports" to "verify the blockchain."
3
GST on Online Gaming and Revenue Transparency
The GST Council's 2023 decision to levy 28% GST on the full face value of bets in online gaming (rather than on gross gaming revenue) has significantly impacted the industry. For operators, accurate tracking of every bet and payout is now a tax compliance necessity. Klethesia's on-chain ledger of cumulative bets and payouts provides an immutable record that satisfies GST audit requirements. Every rupee bet and every rupee paid out is recorded on-chain with timestamps, making tax compliance automatic rather than manual. This reduces the risk of GST disputes and penalties, which have been a major concern for Indian gaming companies since the new rate took effect.
4
KYC and Responsible Gaming Integration
Indian gaming regulations increasingly mandate robust KYC (Know Your Customer) and responsible gaming measures including deposit limits, self-exclusion, and loss caps. MST's smart contract architecture allows these limits to be enforced at the protocol level. A player's deposit limit can be coded into their gaming wallet contract - the chain itself refuses to process bets beyond the limit. Self-exclusion periods are enforced cryptographically: once activated, no game contract will accept bets from that wallet until the exclusion period ends. This is far more reliable than operator-side enforcement, where a determined player might circumvent limits by contacting customer support.
💡 The Regulatory Advantage for Indian Operators

Indian gaming operators deploying on MST gain a significant regulatory advantage. When the SRO or a state gaming commission demands proof of fair play, the operator does not need to open their server code for inspection. They point to the blockchain. Every hand of rummy, every slot spin, every loot box opening is permanently recorded with cryptographic proof of fairness. This kind of transparency is unprecedented in the Indian gaming industry and positions MST-based platforms favourably when applying for licences or responding to regulatory scrutiny.

Three Core Protocols
Klethesia: On-chain RTP enforcement for slots. SatvaShuffle: ZK-verified card shuffling. FortunaX: Distributed randomness beacon using threshold BLS signatures.
02
FortunaX = Threshold BLS Randomness
Validators contribute random shares; a threshold (2/3) aggregates them into a single unpredictable random number. No single validator can bias the output.
03
SatvaShuffle = Commitment + ZK Proof + Reveal
Merkle root commitment before hand, ZK-SNARK proof of valid permutation, card reveal via Merkle proofs, full deck verification after hand.
04
Klethesia = Statistical RTP Enforcement
Advertised RTP registered on-chain; contract tracks cumulative payouts and flags or freezes if actual RTP deviates beyond the allowed statistical band.
05
Protocol-Level, Not Application-Level
Fairness is built into the MST chain itself. Game developers inherit fairness guarantees; they cannot bypass them even with malicious contracts.

Key Terms - Sub-Module 7.6

Klethesia
MST's protocol for on-chain RTP (Return-to-Player) enforcement for slot machines, with statistical verification over epochs.
SatvaShuffle
MST's ZK-verified card shuffling protocol that commits to a deck order and proves it is a valid permutation without revealing cards.
FortunaX
MST's distributed randomness beacon using threshold BLS signatures from validators to produce unpredictable numbers.
RTP (Return-to-Player)
The percentage of total bets that a slot machine returns to players as winnings over its lifetime, e.g., 96.5%.
Merkle Root
A cryptographic commitment to a data set; changing any element changes the root, allowing efficient verification of inclusion.
ZK-SNARK
A zero-knowledge proof that is succinct and non-interactive; used by SatvaShuffle to prove a shuffle is a valid permutation.
Threshold BLS Signature
A signature scheme where multiple parties contribute shares; when a threshold is reached, a single aggregate signature is produced.
Commitment Scheme
A cryptographic primitive where a value is hidden but locked (committed) at one time, and revealed later, preventing changes after commitment.
Statistical Enforcement
Klethesia's method of ensuring RTP compliance over many spins, rather than per spin, with automated flagging on deviations.
Provably Fair
A system where players can independently verify game outcomes using cryptographic proofs, without trusting the operator.
Verifiable Random Function (VRF)
A cryptographic function that produces a random output along with a proof that the output was correctly computed from a given input and secret key. The holder of the secret key can compute the output and proof, while anyone with the public key can verify correctness without learning the secret key.
Hash-to-Curve
A cryptographic technique that maps an arbitrary input (like a seed string) to a point on an elliptic curve in a deterministic and uniform manner, used as a building block in VRF computation.
State Channel
An off-chain scaling mechanism where multiple transactions occur between parties without being individually recorded on the main chain; only the opening and final settlement are posted on-chain, dramatically reducing gas costs for high-frequency interactions like rapid game actions.
Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO)
A body established under India's IT Rules (2023 amendments) to oversee online gaming platforms, verify fair play standards, and ensure compliance with responsible gaming norms - replacing purely government-driven regulation with industry co-regulation.
Epoch (in Klethesia)
A configurable window of spins (e.g., 100,000 spins) over which Klethesia measures actual RTP against the advertised rate. Statistical enforcement operates at epoch boundaries to allow for natural variance within shorter time frames.
Randomness Beacon
A service that periodically emits publicly verifiable random values. FortunaX is MST's native beacon, producing fresh randomness every block cycle using threshold cryptography from the validator set.
