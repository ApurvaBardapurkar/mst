02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Lost Passphrase That Changed Everything

📖 Story

Priya, a 24-year-old graphic designer from Pune, received ₹8,000 worth of Ethereum as a wedding gift. Her cousin set up a MetaMask wallet and told her to write down the 12-word seed phrase and keep it safe. "Never lose this, never share it," he said. Priya carefully wrote the words on a piece of paper and slipped it into a drawer.


Two years later, Ethereum's price surged. Priya's gift was now worth ₹2.8 lakh. She wanted to sell some to pay for a design course. She opened the drawer - the paper was gone. Perhaps it was thrown away during a cleaning spree, or maybe the ink faded. Without the seed phrase, the wallet was irretrievable. The money was still on-chain, visible on Etherscan, but Priya could never move it. She became one of the estimated 20% of crypto users who have lost access to their funds due to seed phrase mismanagement.


Now imagine a different system. Priya downloads an app, taps "Sign in with Google," scans her fingerprint, and a wallet is created instantly. No seed phrase. No paper. No "write this down." Her private key is generated and split across multiple secure enclaves - her phone's Secure Enclave, MST's distributed key-management nodes, and an encrypted backup with her Google account. To sign a transaction, she just scans her fingerprint again. Even if her phone is lost, she can recover the wallet by signing into Google on a new device and completing biometric verification.


That system is SARAL - MST's non-custodial, biometric-based key management protocol. It delivers the simplicity of Web2 login with the self-custody of Web3. Priya would have never lost her funds, and onboarding the next billion users becomes possible.

The seed phrase is the single biggest barrier to Web3 adoption. SARAL removes it entirely, while ensuring users remain in complete control of their private keys - not a centralised custodian.

03   Background & History

From BIP39 to Biometrics - The Evolution of Wallet Security

2013
BIP39 Mnemonic Phrases. Bitcoin Improvement Proposal 39 standardised a way to encode a private key into 12-24 human-readable words. This made backups possible but introduced a catastrophic single point of failure: lose the words, lose everything. No customer support, no "forgot password."
2018
Hardware Wallets. Ledger and Trezor popularised storing private keys on dedicated secure hardware, isolated from the internet. While more secure against malware, they still relied on seed phrase backups and added cost and friction for casual users.
2020
Multi-Party Computation (MPC) Wallets. Fireblocks and others split a private key into shares held by different parties, requiring collaboration to sign. This eliminated single-point key compromise but often introduced a trusted third party and was not user-friendly for retail.
2022
Passkeys & WebAuthn. Apple, Google, and Microsoft adopted FIDO2/WebAuthn standards, enabling biometric-based passwordless logins. The cryptographic keys were stored in device secure enclaves and synced across devices. This provided the UX foundation for SARAL's approach.
2024 - Today
SARAL Protocol. MST combines threshold signatures, secure enclaves, and OAuth (Google/Apple) to create a self-custodial wallet where the user's biometrics and social login enable key generation, recovery, and transaction signing - without any party ever holding the full key. Seed phrases are eliminated for good.
💡 The SARAL Philosophy

"SARAL" means "simple" in Hindi. The protocol's goal: make Web3 wallet creation as simple as signing into a website, while preserving the self-custody that makes blockchain valuable.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Safe Deposit Box Analogy - How SARAL Works

🏦 The Three-Key Safe

Imagine a bank safe deposit box that requires three keys to open. You hold one key (your phone's biometric), the bank holds a second key (MST's key-management node), and your lawyer holds a third key (your Google account's encrypted backup). To open the box (sign a transaction), you need any two of the three keys. If you lose your phone, you can still open the box with the bank's key and your lawyer's key - but nobody can open it alone. This is threshold cryptography, the core of SARAL.

W
WHY
Why does SARAL exist?
Because seed phrases are a UX disaster. 20% of crypto users have lost funds permanently. For Web3 to reach a billion users, wallet creation must be as simple as "Sign in with Google" - with no risk of permanent loss and no trusted custodian holding the keys.
W
WHAT
What exactly does SARAL do?
SARAL is a non-custodial key management protocol. It uses the user's biometric (fingerprint/face), a social login (Google/Apple), and a distributed network of MST key-management nodes to generate, store, and use a blockchain private key. The full key never exists in one place; signing requires a threshold of shares.
H
HOW
How does the technical flow work?
1) User authenticates with Google → an OAuth token is obtained. 2) The SDK sends a request to a SARAL key-management node. 3) The node, the user's device secure enclave, and the OAuth provider's key sync service cooperatively perform a distributed key generation (DKG) to create shares of an ECDSA key. 4) The user's public key (MST address) is registered on-chain. 5) To sign a transaction, the user re-authenticates with biometrics, the device share and the node share jointly produce a signature via threshold ECDSA. No single party ever holds the full private key.
W
WHERE
Where is it used?
SARAL powers the BridgeKey wallet - MST's flagship wallet. It can be integrated by any dApp using the SARAL SDK, enabling one-click wallet creation and transaction signing from web and mobile apps. It's especially impactful for gaming, social, and remittance dApps targeting non-technical users.
L
LIMITS
What are its limitations?
SARAL relies on the security of the user's Google/Apple account and their device's biometric sensor. If both are simultaneously compromised, the attacker could initiate a recovery. Also, the key-management nodes are a semi-trusted infrastructure; while they cannot sign alone, they could collude with an OAuth provider. MST mitigates this by decentralising the key-management nodes across multiple independent operators and requiring additional authentication factors for high-value transactions.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The Cryptography of SARAL - Threshold ECDSA & Distributed Key Generation

SARAL's magic relies on two advanced cryptographic techniques: Distributed Key Generation (DKG) and Threshold ECDSA Signing. Understanding these reveals why SARAL is non-custodial despite never showing the user a full private key.

1
Distributed Key Generation (DKG) - Creating a Key Without Creating It
In traditional key generation, a single device creates a private key and then splits it. This is insecure: one device briefly holds the full key. SARAL uses Distributed Key Generation: the user's device, the MST key-management node, and a recovery service (tied to the OAuth account) run a multi-party protocol that outputs shares of an ECDSA private key. The full private key never exists at any point in time on any device. Each party holds one share, and no single share can sign a transaction. The protocol ensures that the resulting public key (and thus the wallet address) is computed without anyone knowing the private key.
2
Threshold ECDSA - Signing with Shares
ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) is used by Ethereum and MST for transaction signing. Standard ECDSA requires a single private key. Threshold ECDSA allows a group of parties, each holding a share of the key, to collaboratively produce a valid signature without ever combining their shares. In SARAL, the user's device (using biometric-unlocked secure enclave) and the MST key-management node jointly run the threshold signing protocol. A 2-of-3 threshold is used: any two of the three shares (device, node, recovery service) can sign. This means if the user loses their phone, the node + recovery service can sign a recovery transaction to move funds to a new key.
3
Secure Enclave & Biometric Binding
On mobile devices, the key share is stored in the Secure Enclave (iOS) or StrongBox (Android) - a hardware-isolated coprocessor that requires biometric authentication to access. The share never leaves the enclave in plaintext. When signing, the enclave performs its part of the threshold computation internally and outputs only the partial signature. This prevents malware from extracting the key share even if the device is compromised.
4
OAuth-Based Recovery
The third share is encrypted and stored in the user's cloud account (Google Drive / iCloud) via the OAuth token. During recovery, the user signs into Google on a new device, the SARAL SDK retrieves the encrypted share, and together with the MST node's share, it performs threshold signing to authorise a key migration. The old device's share is not needed. This provides a familiar "forgot password" recovery flow while remaining non-custodial - Google cannot decrypt the share, and the MST node cannot act alone.
5
Key-Management Nodes - Decentralised & Audited
MST operates a network of key-management nodes run by independent validators. These nodes participate in DKG and threshold signing. They are economically incentivised to behave honestly (they stake MSTC, subject to slashing for misbehaviour). The threshold signing protocol ensures that even if a subset of nodes is compromised, user funds remain safe as long as the threshold is not met. Users can also choose to run their own recovery service for maximum self-sovereignty.
🗝️ The Non-Custodial Guarantee

At no point does any single entity - not Google, not the MST node, not the user's device - hold the full private key. Transaction signing requires cooperation of the user (via biometric) and the network. This is self-custody without seed phrases.

05A   Mathematics of Threshold Cryptography

Shamir's Secret Sharing, DKG & Polynomial Interpolation

To truly understand why SARAL is secure, we need to explore the mathematical foundations. Threshold cryptography is built on elegant number theory - specifically, the properties of polynomials over finite fields. Let us walk through the two key approaches: Shamir's Secret Sharing (SSS) and Distributed Key Generation (DKG), understanding why SARAL chose DKG over the simpler SSS.

1
Shamir's Secret Sharing (SSS) - The Foundation

Invented by Adi Shamir in 1979, this scheme splits a secret into n shares such that any t shares (the threshold) can reconstruct the secret, but t-1 shares reveal nothing. It relies on a fundamental property of polynomials: a polynomial of degree t-1 is uniquely determined by t points on its curve.

How it works (simplified): Suppose you want a 2-of-3 scheme (like SARAL). You have a secret s (the private key). You create a random polynomial of degree 1 (a straight line): f(x) = s + a*x, where a is a random number. Then you evaluate f(1), f(2), f(3) to get three shares. Any two shares can reconstruct the line (and thus s = f(0)), but one share alone reveals nothing about s.

2
A Numerical Example of 2-of-3 Sharing

Let us work through a concrete example (using small numbers for clarity; real SARAL uses 256-bit values):

Step 1 - Choose the secret: Let the secret (private key) be s = 7.

Step 2 - Create the polynomial: For a 2-of-3 threshold, we need degree 1: f(x) = 7 + 3x (where 3 is random).

Step 3 - Generate shares:

  • Share 1 (Device): f(1) = 7 + 3(1) = 10
  • Share 2 (MST Node): f(2) = 7 + 3(2) = 13
  • Share 3 (Cloud Backup): f(3) = 7 + 3(3) = 16

Step 4 - Reconstruction with any 2 shares: Given shares (1, 10) and (2, 13), use Lagrange interpolation to find f(0):

f(0) = 10 * (0-2)/(1-2) + 13 * (0-1)/(2-1) = 10 * 2 + 13 * (-1) = 20 - 13 = 7

The secret is recovered. With only one share, there is no information about the other coefficient, so the secret remains hidden.

3
Lagrange Interpolation - The Recovery Formula

The mathematical formula used to recover the secret from t shares is called Lagrange interpolation. For points (x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), ..., (x_t, y_t), the polynomial value at x=0 is:

f(0) = sum of [ y_i * product of ( (0 - x_j) / (x_i - x_j) ) for all j != i ]

This formula works in any field - in practice, cryptographic implementations use modular arithmetic over a large prime field (e.g., the order of the secp256k1 elliptic curve, approximately 2^256). The beauty is that this formula can also be applied to partial computations (partial signatures) without reconstructing the secret - which is exactly what threshold ECDSA does.

4
Why DKG is Better Than Simple SSS for SARAL

Shamir's scheme has a critical flaw for wallet creation: someone (a "dealer") must know the full secret first, then split it. For SARAL, this would mean one party momentarily holds the full private key - a security risk. Distributed Key Generation (DKG) solves this:

  • No dealer: Each party generates its own random polynomial and shares evaluations with others. The final key shares are computed by each party independently.
  • Verifiability: DKG uses Feldman's Verifiable Secret Sharing (VSS) - parties can verify that shares are consistent without learning the secret. This prevents a malicious party from sending incorrect shares.
  • The key never exists: The public key is computable from the commitments, but the private key is never assembled in any single location.

Think of it like three artists collaboratively painting a mural: each contributes brush strokes according to a coordinated plan, and the final image emerges, but no single artist ever held the complete design document.

5
Feldman's VSS - Preventing Cheating in DKG

In a standard DKG protocol, a malicious participant could send different values to different parties, corrupting the key generation. Feldman's Verifiable Secret Sharing adds a verification step:

Each party publishes commitments to their polynomial coefficients using elliptic curve point multiplication: C_k = a_k * G (where G is the generator point). Other parties can verify that their received share is consistent with these public commitments using the equation: share_i * G = C_0 + i*C_1 + i^2*C_2 + ...

If verification fails, the dishonest party is identified and excluded. This ensures that even in an adversarial environment, the DKG protocol produces valid, consistent shares.

🎓 Exam Analogy for DKG vs SSS

SSS: A professor writes the answer key, photocopies it, cuts each copy into pieces, and distributes them. The professor briefly held the complete answer key - if compromised at that moment, all answers leak.

DKG: Three professors each write one-third of the questions independently. They share hints with each other to ensure consistency but never see the full paper. The complete exam only exists when students sit for it (when signing occurs). No professor ever held the full answer key.

📐 Key Mathematical Insight

The security of threshold cryptography rests on the information-theoretic property that t-1 points on a degree t-1 polynomial reveal absolutely zero information about the polynomial's value at any other point. This is unconditional security - it holds even against an adversary with unlimited computational power. No quantum computer can break Shamir's scheme.

07A   Security Threat Analysis

What Happens If Each Component Is Compromised?

A rigorous security analysis requires examining what happens when individual components of the SARAL system are compromised. Because SARAL uses a 2-of-3 threshold, the system is designed to withstand the compromise of any single component. However, understanding the threat landscape helps users and developers make informed risk decisions.

⚠️
Threat 1: Google/Apple Account Breach

Scenario: An attacker gains access to the user's Google account through phishing, credential stuffing, or social engineering (e.g., SIM swap attacks common in India where attackers convince telecom operators to issue duplicate SIMs).

What the attacker gains: Access to the encrypted recovery share stored in Google Drive/iCloud.

Can they steal funds? No. The attacker has only 1 of 3 shares. They still need either the device share (requires biometric) or the MST node share (requires authentication). The recovery share alone is mathematically useless - it reveals zero information about the private key.

Mitigation by SARAL: The recovery share is encrypted with a key derived from the device's secure enclave during initial setup. Even with Google account access, decrypting the share requires a secondary authentication factor. Additionally, any recovery attempt triggers a time-locked cooldown period (48 hours) with notifications to all registered devices, giving the legitimate user time to cancel the request.

⚠️
Threat 2: Phone Theft or Physical Device Compromise

Scenario: A user's phone is stolen in a crowded Mumbai local train, or a sophisticated attacker physically obtains the device and attempts to extract the key share from the Secure Enclave.

What the attacker gains: Physical possession of the device containing the device share in the Secure Enclave.

Can they steal funds? No. The Secure Enclave requires biometric authentication (fingerprint or face scan) to release the share for signing operations. After 5 failed biometric attempts, the device locks permanently and the enclave erases the key material. Even sophisticated hardware attacks (decapping the chip, electron microscopy) cannot extract keys from modern Secure Enclaves - Apple's Secure Enclave Processor (SEP) and Google's Titan M2 chip are designed to resist physical probing.

What should the user do? Sign into Google on a new device, authenticate with biometrics, and initiate a key migration. The old device's share is invalidated, and a new DKG generates fresh shares for the new device.

Indian context: India has over 700 million smartphone users, many in areas with high phone theft. SARAL's architecture means phone theft never equals fund theft - a critical design requirement for emerging markets.

⚠️
Threat 3: MST Key-Management Node Compromise

Scenario: An attacker compromises one or more MST key-management nodes through a software vulnerability, insider threat, or supply-chain attack.

What the attacker gains: The node's share for all users whose keys were generated with that specific node.

Can they steal funds? No. A compromised node holds only 1 of 3 shares. The attacker still needs either the user's device share or the recovery share to reach the 2-of-3 threshold.

Additional protections:

  • Node rotation: Key shares are periodically refreshed (proactive secret sharing) so even stale compromises become useless.
  • Distributed nodes: Users' shares are spread across multiple independent node operators. To attack a user, you must compromise the specific node that holds their share.
  • Staking and slashing: Node operators stake MSTC tokens. Detected misbehaviour results in slashing, creating economic disincentives for insider attacks.
  • Hardware attestation: Nodes run in Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs) like Intel SGX or ARM TrustZone, with remote attestation proving software integrity.
🚨
Threat 4: Simultaneous Two-Component Compromise (Critical)

Scenario: An attacker simultaneously compromises both the user's Google account AND the MST node (or device AND node, or device AND Google account).

Can they steal funds? Potentially yes - this reaches the 2-of-3 threshold. This is the catastrophic failure scenario that SARAL is designed to make extremely unlikely.

How SARAL mitigates this:

  • Rate limiting: Even with 2 shares, the signing protocol enforces transaction limits. High-value transactions require additional authentication or a time delay.
  • Behavioural analysis: The MST node monitors signing patterns. A sudden large withdrawal from a new IP/device triggers additional verification.
  • Geographic checks: If the user's last known location (Bangalore) differs dramatically from the signing request's origin (Lagos), the transaction is flagged.
  • Social recovery option: Users can designate trusted guardians (family members) who must co-approve transactions above a certain threshold during recovery.

Probability assessment: Simultaneously compromising a user's Google account (protected by 2FA, Google's security team) and an MST node (protected by TEEs, staking, and independent operation) is orders of magnitude harder than phishing a seed phrase from a piece of paper.

🛡️ Defence in Depth

SARAL employs defence in depth: even if the mathematical threshold is met, additional layers (rate limits, behavioural analysis, time locks, guardian approvals) provide further protection. Compare this to a seed phrase wallet where a single leak of 12 words results in immediate, irrecoverable fund loss with zero additional barriers.

08A   Industry Comparison

SARAL vs Other Web3 Onboarding Solutions

SARAL is not the only project attempting to solve the Web3 onboarding problem. Several well-funded solutions exist in the market - Web3Auth, Magic (formerly Magic Link), and Privy. Understanding how SARAL compares helps appreciate its unique architectural choices and trade-offs.

1
Web3Auth (formerly Torus)

Web3Auth uses a similar MPC-based approach with social logins. It splits keys using a custom Shamir-based scheme across the Torus Network (a decentralised set of nodes), the user's device, and a recovery share. Users authenticate via OAuth, and key shares are reconstructed client-side to produce a standard private key.

Key difference from SARAL: Web3Auth reconstructs the full private key on the client device during signing. This means the full key momentarily exists in memory - a potential attack vector for memory-scraping malware. SARAL never reconstructs the key; signing is performed using threshold ECDSA where shares are never combined.

2
Magic (Magic Link)

Magic uses a delegated key management approach. The user's private key is generated and stored within AWS's Hardware Security Modules (HSMs). Users authenticate via email magic links or social logins, and the HSM signs transactions on their behalf.

Key difference from SARAL: Magic's architecture is fundamentally custodial - the key exists in a single HSM controlled by Magic's infrastructure on AWS. If Magic's AWS account is compromised, or if Magic ceases operations, user funds could be at risk. SARAL's distributed threshold approach ensures no single infrastructure provider holds the complete key.

3
Privy

Privy offers embedded wallets with social login. It splits keys into three shares: one on the user's device, one encrypted and stored by Privy's servers, and one recovery share. It uses a 2-of-3 threshold similar to SARAL.

Key difference from SARAL: Privy's node infrastructure is centralised (run entirely by Privy Inc.), not distributed across independent validators. Privy also reconstructs the full key during signing in some configurations. SARAL's decentralised node network with staking, slashing, and TEE attestation provides a stronger trust model for users who require full decentralisation.

08B   Developer Integration

Integrating SARAL into Your dApp - SDK Workflow & Code Patterns

For developers building on the MST blockchain, integrating SARAL wallet creation and transaction signing into a dApp is straightforward. The SARAL SDK handles all the cryptographic complexity, exposing a simple API similar to familiar Web2 authentication libraries like Firebase Auth or Auth0.

1
Step 1: Install the SARAL SDK

The SARAL SDK is available as an npm package for web apps and as native libraries for iOS (Swift) and Android (Kotlin). Installation follows standard package manager conventions:

# For web applications (React, Vue, Angular)
npm install @mst/saral-sdk

# For React Native mobile apps
npm install @mst/saral-sdk-react-native

# For Flutter apps
flutter pub add mst_saral_sdk
2
Step 2: Initialise the SDK with Your dApp Credentials

Every dApp registers with the SARAL developer portal and receives a client ID. The SDK is initialised with this ID, which determines the OAuth redirect URIs and rate limits.

import { SaralSDK } from '@mst/saral-sdk';

const saral = new SaralSDK({
  clientId: 'your-dapp-client-id',
  network: 'mainnet',       // or 'testnet'
  authProviders: ['google', 'apple'],
  biometricRequired: true,  // enforce biometric for signing
  recoveryConfig: {
    timeLockHours: 48,
    guardians: []            // optional social recovery
  }
});
3
Step 3: Trigger Wallet Creation (One-Tap Onboarding)

When a new user arrives at your dApp, a single function call triggers the entire onboarding flow - OAuth popup, biometric prompt, DKG, and on-chain registration:

// Wallet creation - happens once per user
async function onboardUser() {
  try {
    const wallet = await saral.createWallet({
      provider: 'google'  // opens Google OAuth popup
    });
    
    console.log('Wallet address:', wallet.address);
    console.log('Public key:', wallet.publicKey);
    // Store wallet.address in your app's user profile
    
  } catch (error) {
    if (error.code === 'BIOMETRIC_CANCELLED') {
      showMessage('Please complete fingerprint scan to create wallet');
    } else if (error.code === 'OAUTH_DENIED') {
      showMessage('Google sign-in was cancelled');
    }
  }
}

From the user's perspective, they see: Google sign-in popup, then a fingerprint prompt, then their wallet is ready. Total time: 5-8 seconds.

4
Step 4: Sign Transactions with Biometric

Once the wallet exists, signing transactions requires only biometric confirmation. The SDK handles threshold signing with the MST node transparently:

// Sign and submit a transaction
async function sendTokens(toAddress, amount) {
  const tx = {
    to: toAddress,
    value: saral.utils.toWei(amount, 'mstc'),
    gasLimit: 21000
  };

  // This triggers biometric prompt automatically
  const signedTx = await saral.signTransaction(tx);
  
  // Submit to MST network
  const receipt = await saral.submitTransaction(signedTx);
  console.log('TX Hash:', receipt.transactionHash);
  return receipt;
}

// Sign arbitrary messages (for dApp authentication)
async function signLoginMessage() {
  const message = 'Login to MyDApp at ' + Date.now();
  const signature = await saral.signMessage(message);
  return { message, signature };
}

Step 5: Handle Recovery Scenarios

The SDK provides a recovery flow for users who lose their device:

// Recovery on a new device
async function recoverWallet() {
  const recoveredWallet = await saral.recoverWallet({
    provider: 'google'  // must use same Google account
  });
  
  // New DKG creates fresh device share
  // Old device share is invalidated on-chain
  console.log('Wallet recovered:', recoveredWallet.address);
  // Address remains the same - funds are safe
}

The recovery process takes approximately 10-15 seconds and requires the same Google account used during initial wallet creation, plus biometric verification on the new device.

🧑‍💻 Developer Experience Principle

The SARAL SDK is designed so that a developer who has never worked with blockchain before can integrate wallet functionality in under 30 minutes. No knowledge of elliptic curves, threshold cryptography, or gas mechanics is required. The SDK abstracts all complexity behind familiar async/await patterns that any JavaScript developer already knows.

💡 Integration Checklist for Production

Before deploying a SARAL-integrated dApp to production, developers should: (1) Register their dApp on the SARAL Developer Portal and configure OAuth redirect URIs. (2) Test wallet creation and signing on both Android and iOS devices. (3) Implement error handling for all SDK methods (network failures, biometric timeouts, cancelled OAuth). (4) Set up webhook listeners for recovery events so your backend can update user state. (5) Configure transaction limits appropriate for your use case.

10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 7.4

SARAL Protocol
MST's non-custodial key management system using biometrics, OAuth, and threshold cryptography to eliminate seed phrases.
Threshold ECDSA
A multi-party protocol allowing a group to jointly produce an ECDSA signature without any single party holding the full private key.
Distributed Key Generation (DKG)
A cryptographic protocol where multiple parties cooperatively generate shares of a key; the full key never exists at any point.
Secure Enclave
Hardware-isolated coprocessor on mobile devices that stores cryptographic keys and performs operations only after biometric authentication.
OAuth
An open standard for access delegation, used by SARAL for "Sign in with Google/Apple" to authenticate the user and store encrypted recovery share.
Key-Management Node
An MST network node that holds one share of the user's key and participates in threshold signing. Operated by independent validators.
2-of-3 Threshold
Any two out of three shares are sufficient to sign a transaction. Enables recovery (node + cloud share) without the original device.
SARAL SDK
The developer toolkit for integrating SARAL into dApps. Provides methods like createWallet and signTransaction.
Biometric Prompt
A system-level authentication dialog (fingerprint or face scan) required to unlock the secure enclave for key operations.
Recovery Share
The encrypted key share stored in the user's cloud account, enabling wallet recovery on a new device without the original device.
Shamir's Secret Sharing (SSS)
A cryptographic scheme (1979) that splits a secret into n shares where any t shares can reconstruct it, based on polynomial interpolation over finite fields.
Lagrange Interpolation
A mathematical formula to reconstruct a polynomial from a set of points. Used in threshold schemes to recover the secret (or compute partial signatures) from share values.
Feldman's VSS
Verifiable Secret Sharing scheme that adds public commitments to Shamir's scheme, allowing parties to verify share correctness without learning the secret.
Proactive Secret Sharing
A technique where key shares are periodically refreshed (re-randomised) without changing the underlying secret, invalidating any previously compromised shares.
Trusted Execution Environment (TEE)
A secure area within a processor (e.g., Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone) that guarantees code and data confidentiality and integrity, used by MST key-management nodes.
Time-Locked Recovery
A security mechanism where wallet recovery requests are delayed by a configurable period (e.g., 48 hours), giving legitimate users time to cancel unauthorised attempts.
