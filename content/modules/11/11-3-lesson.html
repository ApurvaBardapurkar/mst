03   Background & History

From Pawn Shops to Smart Contracts - The Evolution of Lending

Lending is one of the oldest financial activities. Traditional models require trust: a bank trusts you'll repay based on your credit score; a pawn shop trusts the gold you leave behind. DeFi lending takes the pawn shop model and automates it with smart contracts - but instead of gold, you deposit crypto assets as collateral. The smart contract enforces the rules: if your collateral value drops too low, your position is automatically liquidated (sold) to repay the loan. No lawyers, no collection agencies, just code.

The first major DeFi lending protocol was Compound, launched in 2018 on Ethereum. Compound introduced the concept of cTokens - when you deposit ETH, you receive cETH in return, which automatically accrues interest. Compound's interest rates are determined by supply and demand, governed by a utilization-based curve: as more of a pool is borrowed, the interest rate increases to attract more deposits and discourage further borrowing.

Aave, launched in 2020, built on Compound's foundation and added innovations: flash loans (uncollateralized loans repaid within a single transaction), variable and stable interest rates, and aToken (interest-bearing tokens similar to cTokens). Aave also introduced health factors - a numerical representation of your loan's safety, making it easy to monitor.

Today, Compound and Aave are deployed across multiple EVM chains. On MST, with its full EVM compatibility, these protocols can run with identical code - but with gas fees 100,000x lower. This transforms lending from a whale's game (where only large positions justify the gas) into an inclusive financial tool where anyone can deposit ₹100 and earn interest.

Key insight: In DeFi lending, code is the credit check. Your collateral - not your identity - secures the loan. This makes lending permissionless, global, and accessible to anyone with crypto.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Pawn Shop Analogy - How DeFi Lending Works

🏪 The Analogy

Imagine a pawn shop run by a robot. You hand the robot a gold necklace worth ₹20,000. The robot inspects it, says "I'll lend you ₹10,000 against this, at 5% interest." You agree. The robot locks the necklace in a safe and gives you ₹10,000 cash. If you repay the ₹10,000 plus interest, the robot returns your necklace. If you don't repay, the robot eventually sells your necklace to recover the money. But there's a twist: the necklace's value fluctuates. If gold prices crash to ₹12,000, the robot gets nervous - your loan is now dangerously close to the necklace's value. The robot sends you a warning: "Add more collateral or repay part of the loan, or I'll sell your necklace to protect myself." If you don't act, the robot sells the necklace (liquidation) and keeps ₹10,000 + a penalty, returning any leftover to you.

In this analogy, the robot is the lending smart contract (like Aave or Compound). The gold necklace is your crypto collateral (ETH, WBTC, etc.). The ₹10,000 cash is the loan you take out (in a stablecoin like USDC). The robot's warning is the health factor. The forced sale is liquidation. Let's formalize these concepts:

C
COLLAT
What is collateral?
Collateral is the crypto asset you deposit into the protocol to secure your loan. It must be worth more than the loan (over-collateralization). Common collaterals: ETH, WBTC, AAVE tokens, stablecoins. The protocol tracks the value of your collateral in real time using decentralized price oracles (like Chainlink). You can deposit multiple types of collateral in a single position.
L
LTV
What is Loan-to-Value (LTV)?
The Loan-to-Value ratio determines how much you can borrow against your collateral. For example, if ETH has a maximum LTV of 80%, you can borrow up to 80% of your ETH's value. If you deposit ₹1,00,000 worth of ETH, you can borrow up to ₹80,000. But you should never borrow the maximum - a small price drop would trigger liquidation. Most users keep their LTV well below the maximum.
H
HEALTH
What is the Health Factor?
Aave calculates a health factor for your position. It's a single number that represents your loan's safety:
Health Factor = (Collateral Value x Liquidation Threshold) / Borrowed Value
A health factor above 1 means you're safe. Below 1, your position can be liquidated. The higher the number, the safer you are. Example: if your health factor is 2.5, your collateral could drop 60% before liquidation.
Q
LIQUID
What is liquidation?
Liquidation is the forced sale of your collateral to repay your loan when your health factor falls below 1. A liquidator (a bot or user) repays part of your loan and claims your collateral at a discount (the liquidation penalty, typically 5 - 10%). This incentivizes liquidators to keep the protocol solvent. You lose the liquidated collateral but keep any remaining collateral above the loan + penalty. Liquidation is the mechanism that ensures lenders never lose money - the protocol always has more collateral than loans outstanding.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The Mathematical Engine Behind DeFi Lending

Interest Rate Models - How Supply and Demand Set Rates

Both Compound and Aave use utilization-based interest rate curves. The utilization rate U is the percentage of deposited assets that are currently borrowed:

U = Total Borrowed / Total Supplied

When U is low (few borrowers), the interest rate is low to encourage borrowing. As U increases (more demand), the rate rises steeply - both to discourage further borrowing and to attract new depositors. The rate curve typically has a kink at an optimal utilization (often 80 - 90%). Below the kink, the rate increases slowly; above the kink, it jumps sharply to ensure liquidity remains available.

For example, Compound's ETH market might have:

  • Base rate: 2% (the minimum, even when no one is borrowing)
  • Multiplier: 20% (how fast the rate increases with utilization)
  • Kink: 80% (the optimal utilization)
  • Jump multiplier: 200% (rate increase above the kink)

If U = 80% (at the kink), borrow rate = 2% + 20% x 0.8 = 2% + 16% = 18% APY. If U = 95%, borrow rate = 2% + 20% x 0.8 + 200% x (0.95 - 0.80) = 2% + 16% + 30% = 48% APY. The sharp increase above the kink forces borrowers to repay or depositors to rush in.

cTokens and aTokens - The Interest-Bearing Receipts

When you deposit ETH into Compound, you receive cETH. This is an ERC-20 token that represents your deposit plus accrued interest. The exchange rate between cETH and ETH increases over time. Initially, 1 ETH = 0.02 cETH (at the current exchange rate). Over time, 0.02 cETH becomes redeemable for 1.1 ETH. You don't need to claim interest manually - it's built into the cToken.

Aave uses aTokens (aETH, aUSDC, etc.) which work similarly. Aave also offers the option of receiving interest in the same token or in the protocol's native token (like AAVE) as an incentive.

Health Factor Calculation - Step by Step

Let's compute a health factor for a real scenario. Aarav deposits ₹1,00,000 worth of ETH. The maximum LTV for ETH is 80%, and the liquidation threshold is 85%. He borrows ₹50,000 USDC.

  • Collateral value = ₹1,00,000
  • Liquidation threshold = 85% → ₹85,000
  • Borrowed value = ₹50,000
  • Health Factor = (₹1,00,000 x 0.85) / ₹50,000 = ₹85,000 / ₹50,000 = 1.70

A health factor of 1.70 means the collateral could drop 41% before liquidation (since ₹85,000 / 1.70 = ₹50,000). To find the liquidation price: the ETH price must fall such that collateral value x 0.85 = borrowed value. So collateral value = ₹50,000 / 0.85 ≈ ₹58,824. The ETH price must drop from ₹1,00,000 to ₹58,824 - a 41.2% drop.

06   Flash Loans - Borrowing Without Collateral

The Most Powerful (and Strangest) Innovation in DeFi Lending

Flash loans are perhaps the most counterintuitive concept in DeFi lending. While everything we have discussed so far requires over-collateralization, flash loans allow you to borrow any amount with zero collateral - as long as you repay the entire loan within the same transaction. If you fail to repay, the entire transaction is reverted as if it never happened. The blockchain's atomic transaction model makes this possible: either everything in the transaction succeeds, or nothing does.

Flash loans were pioneered by Aave in January 2020 and have since become one of the defining innovations of DeFi. They are not available in traditional finance because there is no equivalent of atomic, all-or-nothing settlement in the banking system. A bank cannot "undo" a wire transfer if the borrower fails to return the funds within milliseconds. But on a blockchain, this is exactly how transactions work - every operation within a transaction is bundled together, and if any single step fails, the entire bundle is rolled back.

💡 The Analogy

Think of a flash loan like borrowing money from a friend for exactly 10 seconds. You say: "Lend me ₹10,00,000. I'll use it to buy gold from Shop A at ₹4,800/gram, immediately sell it to Shop B at ₹4,850/gram, and return your money plus a small fee - all within 10 seconds." Your friend agrees because the deal is entirely risk-free: if you cannot complete the trade, time rewinds and the money never leaves their wallet. In blockchain terms, the "10 seconds" is a single transaction block, and "time rewinding" is transaction reversal (revert).

How a Flash Loan Works - Step by Step (Aave Example)

Let us trace through an Aave flash loan on MST, step by step, within a single atomic transaction:

1
Initiate the Flash Loan
A smart contract (written by the borrower or a developer) calls flashLoan() on the Aave LendingPool contract, requesting a specific amount of a token - say, 1,00,000 USDC. Aave checks that its pool has sufficient liquidity and transfers the full amount to the borrower's smart contract. At this point, the contract holds 1,00,000 USDC it did not have before.
2
Execute the Strategy
The borrower's contract now holds 1,00,000 USDC and can perform any on-chain action: buy ETH on one DEX where the price is low, sell it on another DEX where the price is high, swap collateral in a lending position, or even liquidate an under-collateralized loan to claim the liquidation bonus. The critical constraint is that all actions must happen within this single transaction.
3
Repay the Loan Plus Fee
At the end of the transaction, the borrower's contract must return the borrowed 1,00,000 USDC plus Aave's flash loan fee (typically 0.09%, which is 90 USDC). Whatever remains after repayment is the borrower's profit. If the arbitrage earned 500 USDC total, the net profit after the fee is 500 - 90 = 410 USDC.
4
Atomic Settlement or Full Reversion
The Aave contract performs a final check: did the borrower return the full amount plus fee? If yes, the transaction completes successfully and the profit stays with the borrower. If no - perhaps the arbitrage opportunity disappeared mid-transaction, or slippage consumed the profit - the entire transaction reverts. Every single action within it is undone. The USDC never left Aave's pool. The only cost to the failed borrower is the gas fee for the reverted transaction (on MST, a fraction of a paisa).

11   The Oracle Problem

Why Accurate Price Feeds Are the Backbone of DeFi Lending

Every health factor calculation, every liquidation decision, and every borrowing limit in DeFi lending depends on one critical input: the price of the collateral. But blockchains are isolated systems - they cannot access external data (stock prices, exchange rates, weather data) on their own. They need oracles to bring real-world price information on-chain. If an oracle reports a wrong price, even briefly, the consequences cascade through the entire lending protocol: loans may be issued against overvalued collateral, liquidations may trigger unfairly, or under-collateralized positions may go unliquidated, creating bad debt.

🔮 The Analogy

Recall our robot pawn shop from Section 04. Now imagine the robot is blind - it cannot see the gold market itself. Instead, it relies on a trusted messenger (the oracle) who runs to the gold market every few minutes and shouts back: "Gold is ₹5,000 per gram!" The robot uses this information to decide whether to accept collateral, issue loans, or trigger liquidations. Now imagine a malicious actor bribes the messenger to shout "Gold is ₹50,000 per gram!" The robot, trusting the messenger, would accept vastly overvalued collateral and issue enormous loans that can never be repaid. When the real price is eventually reported, the protocol is left with bad debt. This is oracle manipulation - one of the most dangerous attack vectors in DeFi.

How Oracles Work in DeFi Lending

The most widely used oracle network is Chainlink, which provides decentralized price feeds to protocols like Aave and Compound. Here is how Chainlink delivers price data:

1
Multiple Independent Data Sources
Chainlink aggregates price data from dozens of independent providers - centralized exchanges like Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, and WazirX, as well as decentralized exchanges and professional data aggregators. No single source can unilaterally influence the final price. This is similar to how the RBI calculates reference rates by surveying multiple banks rather than relying on a single quote.
2
Decentralized Oracle Nodes
Multiple independent node operators fetch prices from these sources and submit them on-chain. The Chainlink network uses a median or weighted average of all submissions to determine the final on-chain price. A majority of nodes would need to be compromised simultaneously to manipulate the feed, which is economically and logistically difficult.
3
Heartbeat and Deviation Triggers
Chainlink updates prices based on two rules: a regular heartbeat interval (e.g., every 60 seconds regardless of price movement) and a deviation threshold (e.g., update immediately if the price moves more than 0.5% since the last update). This dual mechanism ensures lending protocols always have a reasonably current price, even during fast-moving or volatile markets.

🇮🇳 Indian Context - The LUNA/UST Collapse and Its Impact

In May 2022, the Terra LUNA ecosystem collapsed catastrophically, wiping out approximately $40 billion in value within a week. Indian investors were significantly affected - WazirX reported that LUNA was among the most actively traded tokens on its platform before the crash, and several Indian crypto communities reported widespread losses. Lending protocols that accepted LUNA as collateral faced a cascading crisis: as LUNA's price plummeted from $80 to near zero within days, oracles updated the price (correctly, in this case), mass liquidations were triggered simultaneously, and some positions became so deeply under-collateralized that even full liquidation could not recover the outstanding debt. The remaining shortfall became bad debt - losses absorbed by the protocol's insurance fund or, in some cases, borne by lenders whose deposits could not be fully withdrawn. This event underscored a fundamental lesson: oracle accuracy alone is not sufficient. The quality and volatility of the underlying collateral asset matter equally.

How MST Mitigates Oracle Risks

MST's architecture provides several structural advantages for oracle reliability in lending protocols:

  • Fast block times: Oracles can update prices more frequently without incurring prohibitive gas costs, significantly reducing the window during which a stale price can be exploited.
  • Low-cost oracle updates: On Ethereum, each on-chain oracle update costs gas. During periods of network congestion (often coinciding with market crashes, when timely updates matter most), oracle operators may delay updates to control costs. On MST, updates cost fractions of a paisa, ensuring consistent and timely price feeds regardless of network load.
  • Reliable liquidation execution: Even when an oracle reports a sudden, sharp price drop, liquidator bots on MST can execute liquidation transactions immediately without competing against gas price spikes. This prevents the accumulation of bad debt that occurs on congested networks where liquidations become unprofitable.
  • MEV resistance: MST's transaction ordering mechanism reduces the risk of oracle front-running, where attackers exploit the delay between an oracle update appearing in the mempool and its inclusion in a block.
Key insight: A lending protocol is only as trustworthy as its price oracle. Before depositing collateral into any DeFi lending protocol, always verify: (1) which oracle network it uses, (2) how frequently the oracle updates, (3) how many independent data sources feed the oracle, and (4) whether the oracle has a proven track record of reliability during market crashes.

New collateral value = 2 ETH x ₹3,00,000 = ₹6,00,000

Debt (with 3 months of interest at 4% APY) = ₹2,00,000 x (1.04)^0.25 ≈ ₹2,01,980

New Health Factor = (₹6,00,000 x 0.85) / ₹2,01,980 = ₹5,10,000 / ₹2,01,980 ≈ 2.52

Result: Priya's position is extremely safe. Her health factor improved from 1.70 to 2.52 - she could now withstand a 60% ETH price drop before liquidation. She has three strategic options: (1) borrow an additional ₹1,00,000 USDC against her appreciated collateral while still maintaining a health factor above 1.5, (2) withdraw some collateral (perhaps 0.5 ETH, worth ₹1,50,000) for personal use while keeping a comfortable buffer, or (3) hold the position unchanged and enjoy the expanded safety margin. In a bull market, DeFi borrowers benefit doubly - their collateral grows while their stablecoin debt remains fixed.

Scenario B: Bear Market - ETH Price Drops 30%

📉 Bear Market

ETH drops from ₹2,00,000 to ₹1,40,000 over three months.

New collateral value = 2 ETH x ₹1,40,000 = ₹2,80,000

Debt (with interest) ≈ ₹2,01,980

New Health Factor = (₹2,80,000 x 0.85) / ₹2,01,980 = ₹2,38,000 / ₹2,01,980 ≈ 1.18

Result: Priya's health factor dropped from 1.70 to 1.18 - still above the 1.0 liquidation line, so no liquidation yet, but dangerously close. Another 15% drop in ETH would push her into liquidation territory. She needs to act immediately. On MST, she has two practical options, both costing under ₹0.01 in gas:

  • Option 1 - Add more collateral: Depositing even 0.3 ETH (worth ₹42,000) would raise her collateral to ₹3,22,000 and her health factor to (₹3,22,000 x 0.85) / ₹2,01,980 ≈ 1.36.
  • Option 2 - Repay part of the debt: Repaying ₹50,000 USDC would reduce her debt to ₹1,51,980 and push her health factor to (₹2,38,000 / ₹1,51,980) ≈ 1.57 - a much safer zone.

Priya chooses Option 2 and repays ₹50,000. The gas for this repayment on MST: ₹0.006. The same emergency top-up on Ethereum during a market crash might cost ₹3,000+ in gas due to network congestion.

Scenario C: Black Swan - ETH Price Crashes 60% in 24 Hours

💀 Black Swan

ETH crashes from ₹2,00,000 to ₹80,000 in a single day (similar in magnitude to the March 2020 "Black Thursday" crash, when ETH fell over 50% in 24 hours).

New collateral value = 2 ETH x ₹80,000 = ₹1,60,000

Debt ≈ ₹2,00,000 (interest over one day is negligible)

New Health Factor = (₹1,60,000 x 0.85) / ₹2,00,000 = ₹1,36,000 / ₹2,00,000 = 0.68

Result: Priya's health factor is 0.68 - well below the 1.0 threshold. Her position is now eligible for liquidation. A liquidator bot on MST detects her under-collateralized position and acts within seconds.

Liquidation math - step by step:

  • The liquidator repays up to 50% of Priya's debt: ₹1,00,000 USDC
  • In return, the liquidator claims collateral worth ₹1,00,000 + 5% liquidation penalty = ₹1,05,000 in ETH
  • At ₹80,000/ETH, that equals 1.3125 ETH taken by the liquidator
  • Priya's remaining collateral: 2 - 1.3125 = 0.6875 ETH (worth ₹55,000)
  • Priya's remaining debt: ₹2,00,000 - ₹1,00,000 = ₹1,00,000
  • New Health Factor: (₹55,000 x 0.85) / ₹1,00,000 = ₹46,750 / ₹1,00,000 = 0.47

The position is still deeply under-collateralized after the first liquidation. A second liquidator may trigger another partial liquidation. In the worst case, Priya loses nearly all of her 2 ETH collateral (originally worth ₹4,00,000 at deposit time, now worth ₹1,60,000 at crash price). She keeps the ₹2,00,000 USDC she originally borrowed, but her net loss is the ETH she surrendered. At the time of the crash, her total loss is approximately ₹1,60,000 (current value of the lost ETH) while she retains ₹2,00,000 in borrowed USDC - so she actually comes out ahead in rupee terms during the crash. However, if ETH later recovers to ₹2,00,000, she has permanently lost the upside on her 2 ETH.

13   Common Pitfalls

Don't Get Liquidated - Avoid These Mistakes

⚠️
Borrowing at max LTV
If you borrow the maximum allowed (e.g., 80% of collateral), even a tiny price drop triggers liquidation. Always leave a comfortable buffer - keep LTV below 50% for volatile assets.
⚠️
Ignoring the health factor
Your health factor can change rapidly in a crash. Use monitoring tools or set price alerts. On MST, you can top up collateral or repay debt instantly for ~₹0.006.
⚠️
Using volatile collateral for stable loans
Borrowing a stablecoin against a volatile asset (like ETH) creates liquidation risk if the volatile asset crashes. Consider using stablecoins as collateral for lower risk, or over-collateralize heavily.

16   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 11.3

Collateral
Crypto assets deposited to secure a loan. Must exceed the loan value (over-collateralization).
Loan-to-Value (LTV)
The ratio of borrowed amount to collateral value. Max LTV sets the borrowing limit (e.g., 80%).
Health Factor
Aave's metric for loan safety: (Collateral x Liquidation Threshold) / Debt. Below 1.0 triggers liquidation.
Liquidation
Forced sale of collateral when a loan becomes under-collateralized. A liquidator repays the debt and claims collateral at a discount.
Utilization Rate
Total borrowed / Total supplied. Determines the interest rate in Compound and Aave.
cToken / aToken
Interest-bearing tokens received upon deposit, representing the deposit plus accrued interest.
Flash Loan
An uncollateralized loan that must be borrowed and repaid within a single atomic transaction. If repayment fails, the entire transaction reverts. Pioneered by Aave in 2020.
Oracle
A service (such as Chainlink) that brings off-chain data (e.g., asset prices from exchanges) on-chain so that smart contracts can use real-world information in their logic.
Bad Debt
Debt that remains in a lending protocol after a borrower's collateral has been fully liquidated but the proceeds were insufficient to cover the outstanding loan. The protocol must absorb the loss.
Reserve Factor
The percentage of interest paid by borrowers that is set aside by the protocol as a reserve (treasury) rather than distributed to lenders. Acts as an insurance buffer against bad debt. Typically 10 - 20%.
Supply APY vs Borrow APY
Supply APY is the interest rate earned by depositors (lenders). Borrow APY is the interest rate paid by borrowers. Supply APY is always lower than Borrow APY because the protocol retains the reserve factor and because not all supplied assets are borrowed.
